How to Sell Sports Gear to Gyms: A Wholesale Pricing Framework

The most common mistake in gym wholesale is pricing by intuition — picking a number that "feels right" and adjusting when gyms push back. The brands that build durable distribution channels build a tiered pricing architecture first, set clear criteria for each tier, and never negotiate on structure. This is that architecture.

By Bill ColbertTreetop Growth Strategy~11 min read

Before You Set Any Price, Set Your Floor

Wholesale pricing starts with a constraint that's easy to skip: your gross margin floor. Before calculating any tier discounts, you need to know the minimum gross margin percentage you can sustain after COGS and before any channel fees, storage, or sales overhead.

For most branded sports gear, that floor is 40%. A product with a 40% gross margin at MSRP means you're manufacturing and delivering the product for 60 cents of every retail dollar. If you offer a 40% wholesale discount, you're giving the entire margin to the gym. If you offer a 45% discount, you're operating at negative gross margin on that channel.

The math that determines your maximum discount

If your COGS is 35% of MSRP (65% gross margin), your maximum sustainable wholesale discount is around 50–55% — leaving 10–15% gross margin after the discount. If your COGS is 50% of MSRP (50% gross margin), your maximum discount is 35–40%. Know your floor before you build any tier above it.

The Four-Tier Wholesale Pricing Model

A durable gym wholesale program uses four tiers, structured by location count and annual volume commitment. Each tier has a specific discount, payment terms, and a minimum annual purchase requirement. The tier structure does two things: it rewards partnership depth with better economics, and it creates a natural upgrade path as gym partners grow.

Payment Terms: When to Use Consignment vs. Net-30 vs. Upfront

Payment terms are where most wholesale programs make avoidable mistakes. The temptation is to offer flexible terms to lower the gym's barrier to entry — but flexible terms without a structure create cash flow problems and bad-debt exposure. Here's the decision matrix:

Situation Recommended Terms Rationale Use?
First order, any new gym 100% upfront or 50% deposit No credit history, no relationship yet. Protects brand against non-payment on first order. Yes
New gym reluctant to commit inventory Consignment (60–90 days) Removes inventory risk for gym; brand takes on risk. Only use when gym has no retail floor experience or is high-fit but hesitant. Sometimes
Established partner, second order+ Net-30 Standard trade terms once credit relationship is established. Require on-time payment history to maintain. Yes
Volume tier partner (10+ locations) Net-45 Larger organizations have longer AP cycles. Net-45 matches their internal process without requiring exceptions. Yes
Gym offering to pay "whenever they can" Do not extend credit Cash flow uncertainty is a red flag for wholesale viability. Redirect to the QR channel, which requires no gym cash outlay. No
Net-60 for any Tier 1–2 partner Do not offer Net-60 is only justified by volume that compensates for extended float. Small gyms don't generate that volume. No

When to Deviate From the Tier Structure

The value of a pricing structure is that you don't negotiate it. Every time you make an exception — "I'll do 47% for this one gym because they're a really good fit" — you undermine the structure for every other partner and create internal confusion about what your prices actually are.

There are exactly two situations that justify a deviation:

Situation 1: A gym requests a tier they haven't technically qualified for

A 3-location gym wants Standard tier pricing. Rather than giving them Standard pricing immediately, offer a conditional upgrade: commit to a 12-month Standard minimum ($5,000) upfront, and we'll price you at Standard from day one. This creates a revenue-secured upgrade path that protects the brand and gives the gym a genuine path to better economics.

Situation 2: A strategic market partner

Sometimes a single gym in a high-value market (e.g., the dominant boxing gym in a top-5 metro) justifies richer terms as a market penetration strategy — not as a revenue play, but as a distribution anchor that unlocks the surrounding market. This is a deliberate business development exception, not a negotiation capitulation. If you make it, document the strategic rationale and keep the exception off your standard rate card.

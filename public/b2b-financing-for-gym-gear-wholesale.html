Seven Financing Structures for Gym Gear Wholesale

Performance gear brands lose wholesale deals not because gyms don't want the product, but because the gym can't write a $10K check this month. Here is the full menu of financing structures, matched to deal size, that remove that friction without giving away margin.

Why Financing Is a Closing Tool, Not a Finance Problem

Most performance gear brands treat payment terms as an afterthought. The sales conversation centers on the product: the quality, the brand story, the margin structure. Then at the close, the gym is handed a $9,900 invoice and given 30 days. A meaningful portion of interested gyms don't convert, and the brand assumes the problem was product fit or price.

It usually isn't. The gym wanted the product. The gym believed the pitch. The gym couldn't absorb a five-figure cash outlay in the middle of a month when they're also paying rent, coaching staff, and equipment leases.

Financing doesn't just help gyms say yes. It tells them something about the brand they're signing with. A gear company that offers flexible payment structures is saying, implicitly, that it believes in the sell-through enough to accept deferred payment. That confidence is contagious. It signals institutional maturity that most boutique gear brands lack.

The core framing shift

Financing reframes the close from "can you write a $9,900 check?" to "can you commit $850 a month to a 12-month Fight Team Program?" Those are very different conversations. The second one almost always wins.

There are seven distinct mechanisms available to a performance gear brand. They are not interchangeable. Different structures fit different deal sizes, different gym financial profiles, and different stages in a brand's distribution maturity. Here is each one, with its costs, tradeoffs, and the scenarios where it wins.

The Seven Structures

1
Milestone Payments (Direct)
Structure the wholesale contract as three payments rather than one: 30% at contract signing, 30% at delivery, 40% at net-60 days post-delivery. The first 60% covers COGS and manufacturing cash. The back 40% lands when member sell-through is underway. No external partner is needed. The gym's payment schedule aligns naturally with the cash their members generate. This lifts close rates meaningfully on Tier 1 deals at zero cost to the brand, apart from bookkeeping and credit exposure on the deferred 40%.
Year-one default
2
Trade Credit Insurance Overlay
Pair the milestone structure above with trade credit insurance from Coface, Allianz Trade, or Atradius. Premium runs 0.2 to 0.5% of invoice value. On a $10,000 deal, that's $20 to $50. If the gym defaults on the back 40%, the insurer pays out 80 to 90% of the invoice. The brand is now offering net-60 terms with near-zero credit risk. This is the cleanest financing play for year one because the cost is negligible, it requires no integration, and it lets the brand extend favorable terms with confidence. Almost nobody in the boutique performance gear space knows to do this.
Pair with Structure 1
3
B2B BNPL (External Financer)
An external partner pays the brand in full at delivery. The gym repays the financer over 30, 60, or 90 days, or up to 12 months. The brand carries zero credit risk. The gym gets time. Fee to the brand runs 2 to 4% of invoice value. Resolve Pay is the right first integration: purpose-built for B2B, Shopify-native, fast gym approval. Add Balance when moving to 12-month annual program deals. The fee should be absorbed by the brand in year one (see the math below). If you pass it through, you undercut the benefit at the exact moment the deal is about to close.
Best for Tier 1 and 2
4
Quarterly Subscription Billing
For annual partnership programs priced at $18K to $30K per year, break billing into quarterly payments aligned to seasonal gear drops. Each payment is $4,500 to $7,500 and coincides with a deliverable: a new seasonal collection arriving at the gym. Cash flow and value delivery sync, which is why gyms accept this structure more readily than an annual invoice. Offer three billing options at signup: monthly auto-debit (lowest friction), quarterly aligned to drops (most natural), annual upfront with a 5 to 6% discount (cash flow leverage for the brand). Default to quarterly. Annual upfront is the "loyalty tier" for gyms that trust the brand after year one.
For annual programs
5
Member Pre-Orders as Financing
The gym runs a two-week pre-order window among its members before placing the bulk order. Members pay 50% at pre-order, 50% at delivery. The gym uses member deposits to fund the brand's required contract deposit. Zero brand credit risk. Zero gym cash-flow problem. The members' own enthusiasm finances the deal. This works particularly well for apparel because members self-select into sizes and colorways, which also reduces the gym's inventory risk. The trade-off: the 3 to 5 week lead time between pre-order and gear arrival requires strong member communication. The fix is a simple SMS or email sequence from the gym to keep members warm during the wait.
Underused and powerful
6
Revenue-Based Financing Referral
For Tier 3 deals ($30K and above), the gym may be better served taking a revenue-based loan from Capchase, Pipe, or Shopify Capital and using the proceeds to fund the brand program. The brand does not underwrite this. The brand has a referral relationship with the financier. Small referral fee back to the brand. The gym repays the financer as a percentage of monthly revenue rather than a fixed payment, which is easier for member-driven businesses with seasonal cash flow. This structure signals to Tier 3 gyms that the brand understands their business at a level most vendors don't.
Tier 3 deals
7
Co-Branded Business Card Partnership
The brand partners with a fintech provider (Brex is the most gym-operator-adjacent) to offer a co-branded card to qualifying gym partners. The gym uses the card to fund the brand program and operational expenses. The brand is paid instantly. The fintech extends the credit. The brand earns affiliate revenue on cardholder spend. This is a year-two move. It requires brand scale, fintech partnership negotiation, and enough gym partners to make the co-branded card a meaningful product. Worth tracking now and building toward once 20 to 30 gym partners are active.
Year two

Matching Financing Structure to Deal Size

The mistake most brands make is treating financing as a single product. They integrate one BNPL provider and offer it across all deals regardless of size. That leaves money on the table on both ends: under-serving Tier 1 gyms who would close faster with simple milestone terms, and under-serving Tier 3 gyms who need more sophisticated structures to justify a $30K annual commitment.

T1
Pilot Gyms: $5K to $10K
Default to Structure 1 (30/30/40 milestones) plus Structure 2 (trade credit insurance). Optionally offer Structure 3 (Resolve Pay) for gyms that prefer an external pay-over-time option. Structure 5 (member pre-orders) is ideal when the gym is willing to run a two-week pre-order campaign. Total cost to the brand: under $100 per deal.
T2
Growth Gyms: $13K to $25K
Structure 4 (quarterly subscription aligned to drops) is the natural fit for annual programs at this tier. Layer in Structure 3 (Balance or Resolve Pay for 12-month plans) for gyms that want full installments. Absorb the fee: at this deal size, the math is clearly positive even at 4% fee cost.
T3
Scale Partners: $30K and above
Structure 3 with Affirm B2B or Balance for long-term installments. Offer Structure 6 (revenue-based referral via Capchase) as an alternative for multi-location operators with complex cash flow. At this deal size, offering better financing terms than Hayabusa or Venum wins deals at price parity.
T4
Year Two: 20+ Active Gyms
Structure 7 (co-branded card) becomes viable once the gym network has scale. The card creates a recurring revenue line from cardholder spend independent of the wholesale program itself. Start the fintech conversation once 20 active partners are signed.

Should You Absorb the Fee?

Yes. In year one, absorb it. The math is not close.

Fee absorption math on a $9,900 Tier 1 deal
B2B BNPL fee at 3% $297
Close rate without financing 20%
Expected revenue per pitch without financing $1,980
Close rate with financing (conservative +8pt lift) 28%
Expected revenue per pitch with financing $2,772
Expected revenue gain per pitch $792
Net gain after absorbing fee $495

The net gain per pitch is positive by 2.6x the fee cost. Every deal you close pays for the fee on roughly two deals you close and two deals you don't. Even at a more modest 5-point close rate lift, the math still favors absorption.

Three positions exist for handling the fee. Pass-through (gym pays the 3% on top) adds visible friction at the close. Baked-in (raise contract price by 3%) is invisible but inflates your listed pricing. Absorption is cleanest: quoted price holds, you pay the fee, close rate goes up. In year two, once the program has proven sell-through data and renewal rates, you can revisit whether absorption or price adjustment makes more sense at scale.

Financing as a Competitive Positioning Tool

Here is the insight most brands miss: financing is not just a closing mechanic. It is a positioning signal.

When a premium performance gear brand offers flexible payment terms to a Tier 3 gym that Hayabusa or Venum did not, the brand wins on terms even at price parity. The signal is unmistakable:

"
We believe in your sell-through
A brand that offers net-60 or a 12-month installment program is implicitly saying it trusts the gym's ability to move product. That confidence is contagious. Gyms want to partner with brands that back their partnership with more than a handshake.
"
We're set up as a partner, not a vendor
Vendors ask for a check. Partners structure deals around how the gym actually operates. Gyms run on member revenue, not reserve capital. A payment structure that matches their cash cycle signals that the brand has done the thinking vendors skip.
"
We have institutional sophistication
Boutique gear brands rarely offer financing. When one does, it immediately separates itself from the rest of the pitch stack. The Tier 3 decision shifts from "do I want Brand A vs. Brand B?" to "Brand B makes the deal work with my cash flow. Brand A wants a check." Brand B wins.

This dynamic is strongest in markets where the gear brand is not the category-defining name. Against an incumbent with superior brand recognition, payment structure flexibility can be the margin that closes the deal.

The Parallel Member Layer

Everything above addresses the brand-to-gym transaction. There is a parallel financing layer at the member level that runs independently and is worth activating at the same time.

When a gear brand runs a DTC-fulfillment model for gym partners (the brand hosts a co-branded landing page and ships directly to members), members are buying individual units at $50 to $90 each. Consumer BNPL at checkout lifts average order value 10 to 30% on apparel categories. Affirm, Klarna, and Afterpay should be enabled on those member-facing pages.

The average order value lift from consumer BNPL on apparel is well-documented: members who might buy one item buy two when they can pay over six weeks. This is not a complex integration. Most Shopify themes support it natively. The point is to ensure that the co-branded gym pages specifically have it enabled, not just the main DTC product pages.

The question to bring to any DTC or ecommerce conversation

Are BNPL options (Affirm, Klarna, Afterpay) enabled at checkout on the gym partner landing pages specifically, not just on the main store? Member pages often get missed when the main store is already configured.

Implementation Sequence for Year One

You do not need to implement all seven structures at once. The right sequence for a gear brand building this channel from scratch:

Month one: Draft the milestone payment contract language (30/30/40). Add trade credit insurance for deals over $5K. These require no integrations. Every deal you write from day one should use this structure.

Month two: Integrate Resolve Pay for buyers who prefer an external pay-over-time option. Set up the Shopify integration. Configure the fee to be absorbed by the brand in the contract pricing. Enable consumer BNPL (Affirm or Klarna) on all gym partner landing pages.

Month three and beyond: For multi-location or annual program deals, introduce quarterly billing aligned to seasonal drops. Evaluate Balance as a second provider for 12-month installment deals. Begin the Capchase referral conversation if Tier 3 prospects are in the pipeline.

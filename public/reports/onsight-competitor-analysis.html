OnSight: Equipment Intelligence Is Not Member Intelligence

Quick Answer

OnSight (onsight-tech.com) tracks gym equipment usage and space occupancy through physical sensors. It tells gym managers which machines are busy and when, not which members are about to leave. The two products solve different problems. If your revenue leak is member churn, equipment sensors do not fix it.

What OnSight Is

OnSight positions itself as "Equipment Intelligence For Gyms." The product is hardware-led: physical sensors attach to or near gym equipment and transmit real-time usage data to a cloud analytics dashboard. According to the company's own homepage, the core promise is to "track gym equipment usage and occupancy in real-time" so that gym managers can "optimize operations with sensor-powered analytics."

The product surfaces answers to operational questions: which treadmills are running at capacity, which zones of the gym floor are underused, when peak utilization windows occur, and whether specific machines are sitting idle during high-traffic hours. These are legitimate operational questions, particularly for large-format fitness facilities deciding where to deploy capital on equipment refresh cycles or floor reconfigurations.

The Hardware-First Model

OnSight's differentiation is physical instrumentation. Unlike software-only platforms that pull data from existing gym management system check-ins, OnSight installs sensors independently of whatever management software the gym already runs. This makes it equipment-agnostic in theory: it does not require smart equipment from Technogym or Life Fitness to capture usage data.

The trade-off is deployment friction and per-location capital cost. Every facility requires physical sensor installation. Scaling to a multi-location portfolio multiplies both the hardware investment and the maintenance surface area.

What OnSight Measures

Equipment Layer

Which machines are in use, for how long, and how frequently. Utilization rates by equipment type. Peak usage windows by time of day and day of week.

What OnSight Measures

Space Layer

Zone-level occupancy across the gym floor. Crowd density patterns. Flow between functional areas (cardio, weights, stretching zones).

Primary Use Cases

Operational Decisions

Equipment purchasing and retirement decisions. Floor layout optimization. Maintenance scheduling. Staff allocation to high-traffic areas.

What OnSight Is Not

The boundary of OnSight's value proposition is clearly defined by what it does not track: members. Equipment sensors measure machine utilization and zone occupancy. They do not know which member is using a piece of equipment. They do not track whether that member has been visiting less frequently over the past month. They cannot identify that a member who used to come four times a week now comes once, and flag that pattern as a churn signal.

This is not a product gap unique to OnSight. It is a structural limit of equipment-layer analytics. The sensor on the treadmill knows the treadmill is running. It does not know the person running on it is reconsidering their membership.

The retention gap: The HFA 2025 Benchmarking Report found that fitness operators lose approximately one-third of their member base every 12 months. Nearly half of all new members disengage within six months. The primary driver is behavioral disengagement, not equipment availability. A gym with perfectly optimized equipment placement can still hemorrhage members because no one is watching the behavioral signals that precede cancellation.

The Question OnSight Cannot Answer

The most financially consequential question in fitness operations is: "Which of my current members is about to leave?" OnSight cannot answer it. Nor can any equipment-layer sensor platform, because churn prediction is a behavioral data problem. It requires member-level tracking: check-in frequency trends, class engagement drop-off, booking cancellation patterns, and the delta between a member's historical visit behavior and their current pattern.

Scale that across dozens or hundreds of at-risk members per month, and the revenue impact of behavioral analytics dwarfs the savings from better equipment placement.

OnSight's Content Strategy: What the Blog Does (and Does Not) Cover

OnSight's blog (onsight-tech.com/blogs) operates as a content layer for a hardware-focused product. The site is built as a JavaScript single-page application, which creates immediate indexability challenges for organic search. SPA-rendered content requires search engine crawlers to execute JavaScript before indexing, a scenario that Google handles inconsistently and Bing, AI crawlers, and LLM training pipelines handle poorly or not at all.

Based on what is publicly available about OnSight's product positioning, their content likely focuses on equipment utilization ROI, gym floor optimization, and sensor deployment case studies. These are legitimate topic clusters but represent a narrow slice of the conversations fitness operators are actually having in 2026.

The Strategic Gap OnSight Cannot Close

OnSight's product architecture creates a permanent boundary: because their sensors measure machines and zones rather than members, they cannot generate member-level behavioral intelligence. This is not a feature they could add without fundamentally changing their product. Bridging the gap would require either camera-based member identification (which raises serious privacy concerns and regulatory risk) or deep integration with gym management system member data (which would make them a software-layer platform rather than a hardware-sensor company).

Strategic Implication

The market for "gym analytics" contains two distinct buyer problems. Problem one: operational efficiency and capital allocation for equipment. Problem two: member retention and revenue protection. OnSight owns problem one. Problem two remains wide open for platforms that can read member behavioral signals, surface churn risk early, and trigger intervention workflows before cancellations happen.

The second problem is also larger in revenue impact. Gym operators spending $10,000 on sensor hardware to optimize equipment placement are making a one-time capital decision. Gym operators losing 33% of their member base annually are watching revenue leave through a recurring leak. The urgency and ROI story for member intelligence is structurally stronger.

Where the Two Can Coexist

Equipment intelligence and member intelligence are not inherently competitive. A fitness operator can run OnSight sensors to inform equipment purchasing decisions while also running a member behavioral analytics layer to predict and prevent churn. The market is not zero-sum at the facility level. At the vendor level, however, the positioning battle is real: operators have limited technology budgets, and a platform that leads with member retention ROI will typically win budget conversations ahead of one that leads with equipment utilization.

Questions Fitness Operators Are Asking in 2026

The following Q&A addresses the most common questions gym operators and technology buyers raise when evaluating OnSight and the broader gym analytics category.

What does OnSight do for gym managers?

OnSight provides real-time equipment usage tracking and zone occupancy data through physical sensors. Gym managers use the resulting dashboard to understand which equipment is most utilized, identify idle machines during peak hours, plan maintenance schedules based on actual usage rather than calendar intervals, and make data-informed decisions about equipment investment and floor layout changes.

Is OnSight worth it for a single-location gym?

It depends entirely on the operator's primary pain point. If a single-location gym is struggling with equipment congestion, making a large equipment capital decision, or trying to justify a floor reconfiguration, OnSight's sensor data provides a useful evidence base. If the primary pain point is member churn or declining visit frequency, equipment sensors do not address the underlying problem and the investment may not generate meaningful ROI.

What does OnSight cost?

OnSight has not published pricing publicly. As a hardware-plus-software platform, pricing typically scales with the number of sensors (i.e., pieces of equipment or zones instrumented) and may include a recurring SaaS subscription for the analytics dashboard layer. Operators should request a quote directly from onsight-tech.com and evaluate total cost of ownership including hardware, installation, and ongoing subscription fees against the specific operational problem they are trying to solve.

What is the best gym analytics software for member retention?

Member retention analytics require member-level behavioral data, not equipment sensors. Platforms that provide churn prediction, at-risk member identification, and retention intervention workflows are purpose-built for this problem. These platforms typically integrate with existing gym management systems (ClubReady, ABC Fitness, Mindbody, Daxko) and build predictive models on top of the check-in, booking, and engagement data those platforms already capture. OnSight does not operate in this category.

How is OnSight different from Technogym's mywellness platform?

Technogym's mywellness platform instruments Technogym-branded equipment and provides both member-facing workout tracking and operator-facing utilization analytics. OnSight is equipment-agnostic and hardware-independent of any specific equipment brand. However, both remain on the equipment and facility layer. Neither provides member behavioral intelligence in the churn prediction sense. mywellness does connect workout data to member profiles, which gives it more member-level context than pure occupancy sensors, but it is still not a churn prediction or retention analytics platform.

Bottom Line for Fitness Operators

OnSight answers the question "how is our equipment being used?" It does not answer "which members are about to leave?"

Both questions are worth answering. They require different tools, different data layers, and different vendor relationships. Fitness operators evaluating their technology stack in 2026 should be explicit about which problem they are prioritizing, because the ROI story for each is structurally different.

Equipment analytics optimizes capital allocation for a one-time or multi-year decision cycle. Member behavioral intelligence protects recurring revenue on a continuous basis. For most fitness operators, the second problem is larger, more urgent, and harder to solve without purpose-built tooling.

Treetop Strategic View

OnSight is a well-positioned tool for what it actually does: help operations teams make better decisions about gym floor assets. The fitness technology market would benefit from broader operator literacy about where the equipment analytics category ends and where member intelligence begins. Platforms that can clearly own the member-intelligence category, and build the content and positioning to define it, are competing in a far more defensible and revenue-critical market segment.

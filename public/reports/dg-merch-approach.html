The Approach

DIAGNOSIS BEFORE PRESCRIPTION

A $2M revenue plan is only as good as the inputs it is built on. A plan written without those inputs is a guess in a nice format.

The brief describes the opportunity clearly. The merchandise business is under 3% of revenue, there is real untapped demand, and the mandate is to build, own, and scale it into a meaningful channel. I agree with the read. The opportunity is real.

But the brief also leaves out the variables that actually determine the strategy: how the core business makes money, how often customers come back, how large and how engaged the audience is, who designs the product, and what the client is willing to invest. Those answers do not change the goal. They change everything about how you reach it.

So before the plan, the diagnosis. What follows is the thinking I would bring into the first conversation. Ten questions I need answered, the reason each one moves the strategy, and the four things I already believe before a single answer comes back.

This is not a list of things I do not know. It is a map of the decisions that have to be made on purpose instead of by accident.

ACT I

THE REVENUE FOUNDATION

Merchandise attaches to a core business. Before designing anything, I need to understand the engine the merch line is bolting onto.

01
Maps to — Product & SKU Strategy

What is the core brand actually selling, and what does it stand for in the customer's mind?

What the answer unlocks

Merch is an extension of a brand promise. It has to earn the right to carry that promise.

  • Before designing a single SKU, I need to know what the brand means to the people who already buy it
  • A supplement brand, a combat sports brand, and a training equipment brand each demand a completely different merch strategy
  • The core product defines the lane everything else operates in
02
Maps to — Revenue Ownership

How often do customers repurchase the core product, and what drives lifetime value?

What the answer unlocks

This is the single most important variable for timing the merch program.

  • Monthly repurchase gives twelve natural merch touchpoints a year, at no added acquisition cost
  • A one time core purchase means the merch program has to create its own reasons for customers to return
  • Subscription and consumable brands get merch distribution nearly for free; durable goods brands have to build it deliberately
  • The answer tells me when, and how often, we can put merch in front of the client's customers
03
Maps to — Revenue Ownership

How does the $2M target compare to the brand's total topline revenue?

What the answer unlocks

Context, and a reality check on whether $2M is the right number to aim at.

  • At $10M topline, $2M of merch is a serious 20% business unit
  • At $50M topline, $2M is closer to a rounding error and the ambition should be higher
  • The ratio reveals how much the existing base spends, which informs how much they will spend on merch
  • I cannot size the program honestly without this number sitting next to the target
ACT II

THE WARM AUDIENCE

The existing customer base is the real asset. It is the only thing that makes $2M credible in Year 1, and it has to be sized and reached.

04
Maps to — Channel Strategy & Execution

How large is the customer base, how many are genuinely active, and how is it segmented by engagement?

What the answer unlocks

$2M is warm customers, times the percentage who buy merch, times annual merch spend. I need the first number.

  • Customers are not equal: engaged fans are the launch audience, casual buyers are a later and different motion
  • A brand with 50,000 engaged fans and a brand with 5,000 require different strategies at the same revenue
  • How the base is segmented today is effectively the launch map for the whole program
05
Maps to — Distribution & Go-to-Market

What does the current post-purchase email and SMS cadence look like?

What the answer unlocks

Early merch revenue comes from the warm base, and email and SMS are how we reach them.

  • I need the existing post-purchase flow so the merch message integrates with it rather than competing
  • A strong existing Klaviyo system means inserting merch touchpoints into a working machine
  • No existing flow means building one is itself one of the fastest early wins available
ACT III

THE ATHLETE ENGINE

The brief names athletes repeatedly. In sports merchandise, athlete pull is not a nice-to-have. It is very often the highest converting demand source there is.

06
Maps to — Product, SKU & Marketing Alignment My addition to the brief

Which athletes move the audience, and what is their appetite and contractual ability to be involved in merch?

What the answer unlocks

The role sits inside a brand built on athletes, and athlete anchored product is the highest converting merch in sports.

  • Athlete merch depends entirely on contracts and willingness
  • I need to know which athletes drive engagement, whether they can co-create or endorse, and whether revenue share is expected
  • A favorable answer makes an athlete driven drop calendar the single fastest route to the target
Directly relevant experience I have negotiated partnership deals with professional athletes firsthand, including Sarah McMann of the UFC and Candace Parker of the WNBA. I know how these conversations are structured and where the value is created.
ACT IV

THE BUILD MANDATE

Finally, the constraints. What am I actually being given the room and resources to build, and how does the client think about merch in the first place.

07
Maps to — Product & SKU Strategy

Who owns merchandise design today, and what is the budget for elite external creative?

What the answer unlocks

This is the part of merch that cannot be faked or shortcut.

  • Merch lives or dies on design. A mark someone is genuinely proud to wear is the entire game
  • Creative quality is the moat. AI does not reliably replicate it, and it is not a place to cut corners
  • I need to know what design talent exists internally and what budget exists for elite external talent
  • That single factor separates merch people buy from merch people are handed and never wear
08
Maps to — Distribution & Go-to-Market

What is the client's appetite for investing in product inventory, warehousing, and logistics?

What the answer unlocks

This sets the entire operating model for the program.

  • Willingness to invest in inventory unlocks premium product, stronger margins, and faster fulfillment
  • A capital constrained client points to a print on demand first, low risk approach
  • Neither is wrong, but I need to know which sandbox I am building in before committing to a number
09
Maps to — Product & SKU Strategy

How many merchandise SKUs is the client willing to invest in?

What the answer unlocks

SKU count drives the revenue per customer math, and therefore the path to $2M.

  • A six SKU catalog and a thirty SKU catalog produce very different lifetime value ceilings
  • More SKUs means more reasons to buy and a larger basket, but also more design, inventory, and operational load
  • The right number is a deliberate decision that shapes directly how the $2M model gets built
10
Maps to — Revenue Ownership & Channel Strategy

Does the client view merch as a profit center, or as marketing that customers pay to wear?

What the answer unlocks

A philosophy question that changes every decision downstream.

  • As a profit center: every channel has to clear a margin bar and CAC stays tightly controlled
  • As marketing the customer pays to wear: a near breakeven sale that puts the brand inside a gym for two years is a clear win
  • The answer sets acceptable CAC and the channels we are allowed to use
  • I want it answered early, because the two philosophies build two different businesses

Before The Answers

WHAT I ALREADY BELIEVE

A discovery process is not an excuse to arrive empty handed. These are four working hypotheses I would carry into the role on day one, each one to be confirmed or corrected by the answers above.

HYPOTHESIS 01
The hero product is not a t-shirt.

A t-shirt is a commodity and an add on. The hero product is a performance wearable with identity, something the brand's principal and athletes would wear on camera and a stranger would stop someone to ask about. The t-shirt becomes the entry price point, not the anchor. I will confirm the specific item once I see the product and the audience.

HYPOTHESIS 02
The warm base is the entire Year 1 engine.

Cold paid acquisition for merch is poor economics. The first and largest share of Year 1 revenue should come from monetizing existing trust through email, SMS, and the gym and team network. Paid acquisition is a Year 2 conversation, after the warm base has proven the unit economics.

HYPOTHESIS 03
Merch scales only when it lives in the experience.

When athletes and coaches wear the product where the audience trains and competes, it stops being merch and becomes a uniform. People do not buy a uniform, they buy membership. That presence inside the gym is what makes B2B distribution and organic word of mouth actually work.

HYPOTHESIS 04
B2B is likely the fastest path to the number.

The existing gym, team, and athlete network is a built in retail footprint most brands would spend millions to assemble. DTC is the brand expression layer. B2B is very likely the volume layer, and where I would expect the largest single share of the $2M to come from.

What Happens Next

FROM DIAGNOSIS TO PLAN

Step One
The Answers

The ten questions get answered in the first conversations and through a fast review of the client's data: store analytics, customer exports, email flows, and the athlete roster.

Step Two
The Model

Those answers turn the $2M target from an ambition into a model with real inputs. Channel mix, SKU count, and the revenue per customer math all become grounded numbers instead of guesses.

Step Three
The Plan

Only then does the 30, 60, 90 day plan get written. Built on the client's actual business, which means it is a plan with a real chance of being hit.

Discovery is not a slow process. It runs inside the first two weeks and in parallel with immediate, low risk wins: auditing the existing Shopify store, mapping the gym and team network, and reviewing the current email flows. Momentum starts on day one. The difference is that the plan which follows is built on facts, not assumptions.

Treetop Growth Strategy / Fractional GTM

Fractional GTM

A B2C acquisition engine on Meta, paired with a B2B motion into gyms and studios.

You came to me first about streaming TV. After looking at where the business is right now, the better starting move is a Meta-first B2C acquisition engine with conversion rate optimization sitting underneath it, plus a B2B outbound motion targeting gyms and studios already running Meta ads. This proposal scopes that work. I run it as your fractional GTM lead, not as an agency handing the account to a junior team.

Section 01 / Why this plan

Why Meta-first, with a B2B layer underneath

You originally asked about streaming TV. Streaming is the right channel eventually, but it is a brand-and-reach play that takes 90 days minimum to read signal and works best when there is already a converting funnel for it to feed. You do not have that funnel yet. Building it first is cheaper, faster to read, and protects the streaming investment when you make it.

Here is what I am proposing instead, and why:

  • Meta first. Direct response, daily signal, lowest CAC available to a consumer brand at your stage. We can be live in weeks and reading real conversion data by month two.
  • Conversion rate optimization paired with Meta. Paid media is capped by what happens after the click. CRO is what turns the same ad budget into more customers without raising spend. The two run as one engine.
  • B2B into gyms and studios, layered in after the B2C engine is live. A second growth motion, separate audience, separate playbook. We start with gyms and studios already running Meta ads (visible in the Meta Ad Library) because they have budget, growth intent, and the sophistication to be worth a real conversation.
The two-column reframe: Meta plus CRO is your B2C acquisition engine, selling instructor training and hardware direct to consumers. B2B outreach into gyms and studios is your second engine, selling hardware and training packages to operators. Streaming becomes a real option later, after the B2C engine has produced reliable CAC numbers worth scaling.
What you get

A senior operator running the work directly. I build the Meta account, write and ship the CRO test plan, build the gym/studio prospect list, and write the outreach. You get my hands on the account, not a junior team using my name.

What you don't get

An agency report cycle. A junior media buyer learning on your account. Generic outbound templates with a first-name field. Separate invoices for strategy, execution, and reporting. Work that stalls the moment you stop chasing it.

Section 02 / Service lines

Four service areas, sequenced in this order

Service areas 01, 02, and 03 are one integrated B2C acquisition engine. Meta drives the click, Content fuels it, CRO converts it, and together they produce the lowest CAC possible at your spend level. Service area 04 is the second motion, layered in after the B2C engine is live.

Service area 01 / B2C acquisition
Meta Ads Management
Direct response paid social on Facebook and Instagram. The acquisition channel that drives the click.
What I run
  • Account architecture: account structure, campaign objectives, ad set configuration, audience segmentation. Built for performance from day one, not retrofitted later.
  • Audience strategy: pixel-based retargeting, lookalike builds from customer seed data, suppression lists, and ongoing refresh as the data accumulates.
  • Creative testing: systematic A/B testing of concepts, copy angles, hooks, CTAs, and formats. Rotation cadence set by performance data, not a calendar.
  • Bid and budget optimization: daily monitoring, pacing, CPM and CPC management. Rebalancing across ad sets and campaigns based on real-time performance.
  • Weekly report: spend, impressions, CPM, CTR, CPA, ROAS, CAC trend. Delivered every Monday.
  • Monthly strategy report: full-funnel attribution, creative winner analysis, audience performance breakdown, recommended budget changes for the following month.
Service area 02 / B2C acquisition
Content Direction and Creative Suggestions
The fuel for Meta. Concept and copy direction, not production.
Why this sits next to Meta

Meta is a creative arms race. The algorithm rewards volume and variety, and the brands winning on Meta right now are the ones shipping new concepts every week, not polishing one hero spot. I direct the concept and copy pipeline that feeds the account, and I identify what is already working so we double down before fatigue sets in.

What I run
  • Concept and copy angle development: new ad concepts and copy hooks each month, briefed in a format your designer or video editor can build from directly.
  • Winning-content identification: systematic review of what is converting, why it is working, and which angles to expand into the next batch.
  • Creative testing direction: what to test next, what to kill, and what variant logic to apply (offer vs. hook vs. format vs. proof).
  • Format guidance: static vs. video vs. carousel vs. UGC, matched to where the audience is in the funnel and what the algorithm is favoring this month.
  • Production handoff: direction and suggestions are included. Actual creative production (shooting, editing, design) is outside scope and quoted separately if needed.
Service area 03 / B2C acquisition
Conversion Rate Optimization
The other half of Meta. Media efficiency is capped by what happens after the click.
Why this is paired with Meta

The lowest CAC you will get from Meta is not from better ads alone. It is from Meta and the landing experience working together. I evaluate how the site is impacting Meta ad conversion and fix the on-site bottlenecks so the same paid spend produces more customers. This is the highest-leverage work I do for a B2C brand running paid.

What I run
  • Ad-to-landing message match audit: the creative makes a promise, the page either keeps it or breaks it. I audit every ad against its destination page and flag mismatches.
  • Landing page and funnel conversion audit: step by step, from ad click to confirmed purchase or lead. Every drop-off point identified and ranked by impact.
  • A/B testing program: headlines, hero, offer framing, page layout, CTAs, form fields, social proof placement. Tests sequenced by impact, not by what is easiest to ship.
  • Page speed and mobile UX: the silent CAC killer. Most paid traffic is mobile and most B2C sites lose 20 to 40 percent of clickers to load time and broken mobile experience before they ever see the offer.
  • Lead form and checkout friction: removing required fields that suppress conversion, fixing checkout flow breakage, and rebuilding form UX where it is bleeding paid traffic.
  • Event tracking and analytics: verifying every conversion event fires, that the pixel matches the data layer, and that on-site behavior ties back to Meta cleanly so optimization decisions are based on real numbers.
What you get out of it
  • A documented conversion rate lift target before testing starts
  • A measurable CPA and CAC reduction tied directly to CRO wins, separated from Meta optimization
  • A test log: every test, hypothesis, result, and the page change that shipped
Service area 04 / B2B acquisition
B2B Acquisition Engine: Gyms and Studios
Outbound into a specific market I know well. Layered in after the B2C engine is producing data.
Target market
  • Buyer type: independent gyms, boutique fitness studios, and small chains or franchise groups (2 to 25 locations).
  • Personas: owner, general manager, franchise operator, in-house marketing lead. Outreach is written for the role of the person reading it.
  • I know this market. Treetop has worked with multi-unit operators, franchise systems, and boutique studios across the country. The messaging, the objections, the buying cycle, and the language a gym owner uses are all things I have already learned.
The qualifying signal that changes everything

The primary qualifying signal for the first prospect list is gyms and studios currently running Meta ads, detectable via the Meta Ad Library. This filter is high-fit on three dimensions at once:

  • Active marketing budget - they are already spending, so the conversation is not "should you market" but "can you spend it better"
  • Growth intent - paid acquisition is a deliberate growth move, not a survival posture
  • Sophistication threshold - they understand performance marketing well enough to evaluate a real proposal, which shortens the sales cycle materially

These are the gyms we contact first. Once that list is worked, we layer in adjacent gyms that match on every other ICP criterion but are not yet running paid.

Pitch hook for the outreach
The client sells two things a gym actually buys: hardware that differentiates the training floor, and instructor certification that lets staff teach on it. The outreach ties both to what the gym is already doing on Meta. A studio spending to fill classes has a retention problem waiting on the other side of the funnel. Better equipment plus certified instructors is how boutique operators justify premium pricing and keep members past month three. The opener references their live ads, then connects the hardware and the training directly to the offer they are already running.
What I run
  • Prospect research and list building: pulling gyms and studios from the Meta Ad Library, validating each against firmographic and behavioral criteria, enriching with the owner or marketing decision-maker contact.
  • Personalized outreach: each email references the gym's actual context - their live Meta ads, current offer, market, and what is likely working or not working for them. Not "Hi {{first_name}}, hope you're doing well."
  • Multi-touch sequences across email and LinkedIn: three to five touches per prospect, A/B tested on subject, opening line, and CTA.
  • Response handling: every reply triaged, qualified, and either booked into a meeting or moved to nurture. Interested prospects handed off in a state where the sales conversation can actually start.
  • Reporting: prospects contacted, response rate by sequence, meetings booked, pipeline generated. No vanity numbers.
Section 03 / Process

How the work sequences. B2C engine first, B2B second.

The order matters. The Meta plus CRO engine launches first because it produces measurable signal faster and creates the conversion infrastructure the B2B motion eventually benefits from too. The gym and studio outbound layer activates once the B2C engine is live and pacing.

Phase 01 / Define
Audit and set the baseline
Weeks 1 to 2. Before any dollar is spent.
  • Meta account audit: existing account if it exists, or fresh build. Pixel and event configuration validated, audience seeds prepared, conversion API setup planned.
  • CRO baseline audit: walk the site step by step from ad click to confirmation. Identify every drop-off point. Rank by impact and ease.
  • Analytics check: verify every conversion event fires correctly and that on-site behavior ties back to Meta cleanly.
  • B2B ICP definition: 60-minute working session to lock the gym/studio target profile and confirm the Meta Ad Library qualifying signal. Personas locked.
  • Approval gate: Meta plan, CRO test plan, and B2B ICP signed off before launch.
Phase 02 / Build
Infrastructure, then assets
Weeks 2 to 4. Build in parallel.
  • Meta campaign architecture: account structure, ad sets, audiences, first creative batch QA'd and ready to launch.
  • CRO test plan live: first 3 to 5 tests scoped, hypothesis written, success metric defined for each. Test infrastructure deployed.
  • Conversion tracking verified: end-to-end testing that ad click, page view, conversion, and revenue all tie back correctly.
  • B2B prospect list, batch one: 100 to 200 gyms and studios pulled from the Meta Ad Library, validated, enriched with decision-maker contact.
  • B2B sequence build: three to five touch sequences written, referencing each prospect's actual live ads. Email infrastructure and LinkedIn outreach ready.
Phase 03 / Run
B2C live, then B2B layers in
Month 2 onward. Continuous optimization.
  • Meta live: daily monitoring, weekly creative and bid adjustments, monthly budget rebalancing.
  • CRO test cadence: tests ship continuously. Each winner documented and rolled out. Losers killed without ceremony.
  • B2B outreach activated: gym/studio sequences live, response handling running, meetings booking into your calendar.
  • Weekly combined report: Meta performance, CRO wins, B2B outreach metrics. One report, one source of truth.
  • Monthly call: 60-minute strategy review, decisions on what to scale, what to kill, what to test next.

Section 05 / Measurement

How I measure the work

One metric set per service line. One north star per motion. No vanity metrics, no attribution theater.

Service area 01
Meta Ads (and Content)

Content performance is measured inside Meta. The two report together.

Primary: CPA, CAC, spend vs. budget pacing, CTR, CPM, ROAS where attribution allows.

Secondary: creative performance by variant, audience overlap, frequency, reach by campaign, learning phase exit time.

Cadence: daily monitoring, weekly summary every Monday, monthly attribution report by the 5th.

Service area 03
Conversion Rate Optimization

Primary: conversion rate by page and funnel step, CPA reduction attributable to CRO (separated from Meta optimization), test win rate.

Secondary: page load time, mobile vs. desktop conversion delta, form abandonment rate, checkout step drop-off.

Cadence: test results reviewed weekly, monthly summary of wins shipped and cumulative CPA impact.

Service area 04
B2B Outbound (Gyms / Studios)

Primary: prospects contacted vs. target, response rate (8 to 15% benchmark for well-targeted outbound), positive response rate (2 to 5% benchmark), meetings booked, pipeline value generated.

Secondary: reply rate by touch number, best-performing message variant, ICP accuracy signal (which gym profiles convert).

Cadence: weekly outreach metrics, monthly pipeline review.

North stars: blended CAC trending to your target for the B2C engine (Meta + CRO together), and cost per qualified meeting booked for the B2B engine. If the blended CAC is moving in the right direction, we scale. If not, we diagnose which input (creative, landing experience, audience, ICP, message) is the bottleneck before adding budget.
What I do not measure: email open rates (unreliable since Apple Mail Privacy Protection), ad impressions in isolation, "engagement" metrics that don't connect to conversion or pipeline, or anything else that looks good in a report but doesn't change a decision.
Section 06 / Client obligations

What I need from you

Before we start

  • Meta Business Manager access and admin permission to your existing account, or authority to spin up a new one
  • Website access: CMS, page editor, and the ability to install or update the Meta pixel and conversion events
  • Analytics access: Google Analytics, Shopify, or whatever platform holds your conversion data
  • 60 minutes for a brand and offer working session in week one. We lock the message, the offer, and the positioning before any creative goes out.
  • 60 minutes for a B2B ICP working session in week one or two. We lock the gym/studio target profile and confirm the qualifying signal.
  • Existing customer data (CSV is fine) so I can build the Meta lookalike seed and analyze who your best B2C buyer actually is
  • Designated point of contact with authority to approve creative, page changes, and outreach copy before launch

During the engagement

  • Respond to creative, page change, and outreach copy approvals within 3 business days
  • Weekly 15-minute sync (optional but recommended). These keep the work from drifting.
  • Flag any product changes, pricing changes, inventory issues, or target market shifts immediately. These break campaigns and sequences if I don't know about them.
The single biggest engagement failure mode is approval bottlenecks. Meta creative can't ship without your sign-off. CRO tests can't deploy without page change approval. B2B sequences can't go live without copy approval. The faster approvals move on your end, the faster the engine runs.

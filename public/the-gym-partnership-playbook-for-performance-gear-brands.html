The Gym Partnership Playbook for Performance Gear Brands

Most performance gear brands treat gyms like retail locations. The ones that win treat them like distribution infrastructure — with a revenue model that benefits both sides. This is the complete framework: fit filters, target tier universe, activation mechanics, deal terms, wholesale pricing, and the QR/DTC channel that turns every gym into a recurring revenue stream.

Why Gyms Are Underutilized Distribution Infrastructure

The average independent gym has 350 active members. Those members spend an average of $80 per month on their membership — and, according to industry data, 2.4x that amount per year on gear, apparel, and training equipment. They just don't buy it at the gym.

That's the gap. Gyms have the audience, the trust, and the daily touchpoints. Performance gear brands have the product. Nobody has figured out how to connect them efficiently without turning gyms into retail stores — which they're not, and don't want to be.

The gym partnership model in this playbook isn't retail. It's a three-channel revenue architecture: wholesale (gear the gym buys and resells), a welcome kit mechanic (branded gear every new member receives), and a QR/DTC channel (scan-driven online orders that pay the gym a revenue share without requiring inventory). The gym earns revenue without becoming a retailer. The brand earns distribution without building a field sales team.

Why most brands miss this

The default gym distribution play is "get stocked in the pro shop." That works for shoe brands with national distribution budgets and 60-person sales teams. For everyone else, the welcome kit + QR channel model reaches 10x the gyms at a fraction of the sales overhead — because the gym does the selling for you.

The Four Gym Fit Filters

Not every gym is a viable partner. Before any outreach, run each candidate through four filters. A gym that fails more than one filter is a poor use of activation energy regardless of size.

01
Training Modality Alignment
The gym's primary training activity must match the gear's core use case. A boxing glove brand partners with boxing gyms, MMA facilities, and combat sports clubs — not yoga studios or cycling concepts. Misaligned modality means coaches won't endorse the product, and members have no obvious purchase motivation.
02
Member Spend Capacity
Membership price is a proxy for discretionary income. Gyms with monthly dues above $75 attract members who regularly purchase gear. Gyms below $35/month (big box, budget franchises) attract members who treat gear as a commodity purchase made on Amazon. The former are your partners.
03
Coaching Culture
Gyms where coaches actively program equipment — where the coach says "get a pair of these before next month's class" — convert gear partnerships into sales. Gyms where equipment sits in a corner and members train independently are weaker distribution points. Look for coaches who build product preference into programming.
04
Facility Ownership Stability
Month-to-month lease gyms and owner-operators who are clearly managing cash are risky wholesale partners. A gym that signs a net-30 purchase order and closes six months later has cost you both the inventory and the relationship. Prioritize gyms with their own space, multi-year leases, or franchise affiliation as signals of stability.

The Four-Tier Target Universe

Beyond individual fit filters, gyms cluster into four tiers based on their partnership value and the approach required to activate them. Work the tiers in order: tier one partnerships generate the case studies and testimonials that make tiers two and three easier to close.

Tier 1 — Independent Specialty Gyms

Single-location gyms with a distinct training identity: a boxing club, a jiu-jitsu academy, a kettlebell gym, a CrossFit affiliate. These are your fastest closes and your highest-conversion referral engines. The owner is often the head coach. One phone call, one good meeting, and you can activate a 200-person gym with a welcome kit program and a QR channel running in two weeks. Target 30–50 of these before moving to tier two.

Tier 2 — Regional Fitness Chains (2–9 Locations)

Owner-operated groups that have scaled beyond one location but haven't taken franchise capital. These partners want the economics of a volume deal without franchise bureaucracy. They can move meaningful product volume and, critically, they create natural referral networks between locations. Deal terms need a volume structure (see pricing section) and usually a multi-location rollout plan.

Tier 3 — Franchise Affiliates

Individual operators who own one or several franchise locations of a national concept. The franchise brand sets guidelines, but affiliate owners have purchasing discretion within those guidelines. An affiliate who loves your product can become an internal advocate pushing for brand-level partnership. Franchise affiliate relationships are the door to Tier 4.

Tier 4 — Corporate Fitness Concepts and Hotel Gyms

National brands (fitness concepts with 50+ locations) and corporate wellness centers. These take longer to close, require procurement involvement, and move slower — but a single brand-level deal can place your product in hundreds of facilities simultaneously. Don't start here. Start with tier one, build case studies, and approach tier four with proof.

Five Activation Mechanics

A gym partnership has five ways to generate revenue and brand exposure. Most brands use one. The full model layers all five.

01
Welcome Kit Program
Every new member receives a branded gear item as part of their onboarding. The gym buys kits at wholesale; the cost is embedded into membership or new member fees. This creates immediate product trial at scale — a 50-new-member-per-month gym at $35/kit margin creates $21,000/year in recurring wholesale revenue without any ongoing sales effort.
02
Retail Floor Wholesale
Traditional wholesale: the gym purchases product at a tier-based discount and resells at full MSRP. Best suited for gyms with a physical pro shop or front-desk retail area. Requires minimum purchase commitments and consignment optionality for first-time partners. This is the simplest mechanic but also the most inventory-dependent for the gym.
03
QR/DTC Revenue Share
QR codes on in-gym signage, welcome kit packaging, and product tags link to a partner-attributed landing page on your DTC site. The gym receives 10–15% revenue share on all purchases made through their code, with no inventory required. This is the highest-margin mechanic for the brand and the lowest-friction mechanic for the gym.
04
Coach Endorsement + Social
Coaches receive gear at cost or free in exchange for active endorsement: tagging the brand in training content, recommending specific products in class programming, and featuring gear in member-facing communications. Coach endorsement is the highest-trust signal in a gym environment and the primary driver of member purchase decisions.
05
Event and Competition Activation
In-gym competitions, open mats, charity fundraisers, and member events create high-engagement touchpoints for gear sampling and direct sales. These events concentrate the gym's best athletes in one place — the members who buy the most gear and have the highest social reach. One well-executed event can generate more first-purchase conversions than six months of passive retail.

Deal Term Structure

The deal structure is where most gym partnerships fail before they start. Brands either offer terms too favorable to themselves (gyms say no), or too favorable to the gym (the deal is unprofitable at scale). The structure below is built for long-term durability on both sides.

Initial Commitment

For tier one partnerships, require a minimum 90-day welcome kit commitment or a minimum wholesale order of $500 before extending net-30 terms. This isn't about the money — it's a qualification filter. Gyms that won't commit to a 90-day trial aren't serious about the partnership.

Exclusivity

Geographic exclusivity is the gym's strongest ask and the brand's most valuable concession. For independent gyms: offer a 3-mile exclusivity radius for the duration of the partnership. For regional chains: negotiate by market, not by radius. Never grant category exclusivity — retain the right to partner with other gym types in the same market (e.g., a boxing gym doesn't lock you out of partnering with a CrossFit affiliate in the same zip code).

Annual Review Trigger

Build an annual review into every contract. Review clauses should trigger when either party wants to renegotiate: the gym can move up a pricing tier if they hit volume thresholds; the brand can adjust QR revenue share rates based on scan-to-purchase conversion data. Contracts with no review mechanism become stale and create resentment.

The Wholesale Pricing Model

Four pricing tiers, structured by gym location count and annual volume commitment. Each tier has a specific discount off MSRP, payment terms, and a minimum annual commitment threshold to qualify.

Tier Locations Discount off MSRP Terms Annual Min.
Trial 1–3 40% Net-30 $1,200
Standard 4–9 45% Net-30 $5,000
Volume 10–24 50% Net-45 $18,000
Franchise 25+ 55% Net-60 $60,000 + co-op

One structural note: the first order from any new gym partner should require upfront payment or 50% deposit, regardless of tier. Net-30 terms apply starting on the second order. This is standard practice and no serious gym owner will push back on it.

The QR/DTC Channel Mechanic

The QR channel is where the economics of this model get interesting. Wholesale requires gym inventory and gym cash. The QR channel requires neither — and it generates full-margin revenue for the brand from gym members who never interact with the gym's purchasing function at all.

How It Works

Each gym partner receives a unique QR code linked to a branded landing page on the DTC site. The landing page features a gym-specific welcome message, a curated product selection relevant to that gym's training modality, and a lightweight loyalty hook (e.g., "Members of [Gym Name] get free shipping on your first order"). Every purchase made through that URL is tracked to the partner gym's account.

Revenue Share Structure

The gym receives 12% of net revenue on all QR-attributed purchases. Payouts happen monthly. The gym sees a monthly dashboard showing scans, conversion rate, and payout earned. This transparency is what makes the program feel real — gyms that see scan data and a monthly check become your most active advocates.

Placement Strategy

QR codes go in four places: (1) welcome kit packaging — on the inside of the box or bag so the member scans at home when unboxing; (2) in-gym signage near the entrance and in locker rooms; (3) on hang tags of any retail floor product; (4) in the gym's email footer once they've promoted the program to their member list. The welcome kit placement is consistently the highest-converting because the member is in a purchase mindset and already has the brand in hand.

The key insight on QR economics

A mid-size gym running this model with 300 members scanning at a 4% monthly rate (12 scans/month) converting at 25% (3 purchases/month) at $85 average order value generates $3,060/month in brand DTC revenue. The gym receives $367/month with zero inventory. At a 40-gym network, that's $122,400/month in DTC revenue the brand wouldn't otherwise see — driven entirely by the distribution infrastructure the gym partnerships create.

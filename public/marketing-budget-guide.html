Marketing Budget Guide: How Much to Spend and How to Allocate It

The marketing budget question has two parts most leaders only answer one of: how much total, and how to allocate it. Both matter. Here's the benchmark-driven framework.

By Bill Colbert, founder of Treetop Growth Strategy · Updated June 2026

Marketing budget decisions are made in two directions: top-down (finance gives you a number and you figure out what to do with it) and bottoms-up (you model what results you want, work backwards to what investment produces them, and present the case). The best marketing leaders do both — they accept the top-down constraint as a starting point, then build the bottoms-up model that shows what additional investment would produce.

How Much Should You Spend on Marketing? Benchmarks by Stage

Company StageRevenueRecommended Marketing SpendNotes
Pre-revenue / seed$0$50K-$150K/yrFounder-led content + minimal paid
Early traction$500K-$2M ARR15-25% of revenueHeavy on content, light on paid
Growth$2M-$10M ARR20-35% of revenueBalanced demand gen + capture
Scaling$10M-$50M ARR15-25% of revenueMore efficient, performance-focused
Enterprise$50M+ ARR10-15% of revenueBrand + ABM + large events

These are medians. Companies in competitive markets with high CAC spend at the high end or above. Companies with strong PLG or word-of-mouth engines spend at the low end.

Channel Allocation: Where the Money Should Go

The right allocation depends on your GTM motion and stage. These are typical allocations for a $2M-$10M ARR B2B company running a marketing-led + sales-led hybrid:

Content + SEO25-35%Organic pipeline, brand authority12-18 months to compound

Paid social (LinkedIn)15-20%Awareness, dark funnel conditioning3-6 months

Tools + technology10-15%Infrastructure to execute aboveOngoing

The Bottoms-Up Budget Model

Start with your revenue goal. Work backwards through your conversion funnel to determine the lead volume required, then calculate the investment needed to generate those leads at your current CPL.

Example: $2M new ARR target ÷ $60K average ACV = 33 new customers needed. At a 25% close rate: 132 SQLs needed. At a 45% MQL-to-SQL rate: 293 MQLs needed. At a $450 CPL: $131,850 in demand gen investment to hit the goal. Add 20% for overhead, tools, and brand: $158,000 total marketing budget. This is the model you present to the CEO to justify your ask — not "industry benchmarks say 20% of revenue."

What CMOs Get Wrong About Budget Conversations

The most common mistakes: presenting the budget ask without the revenue model attached, allocating too much to brand and awareness without a clear path to pipeline, protecting existing channel spend because it's familiar rather than because it's performing, and not building in a testing budget (10-15% of total should be available for experiments).

The second most common mistake: accepting a budget that mathematically cannot produce the revenue target, then spending the year trying to explain why results didn't hit plan. If the math doesn't work at the proposed budget, say so in writing before the year starts.

How much should a B2B company spend on marketing?

B2B companies typically spend 15-35% of revenue on marketing, depending on stage and growth objectives. Early-stage companies ($500K-$2M ARR) often spend 20-25% as they build the demand gen engine. Growth-stage companies ($2M-$10M ARR) spend 20-35%. Enterprise companies spend 10-15%. These are medians — competitive markets and aggressive growth targets push budgets higher.

How do you allocate a B2B marketing budget?

For a B2B company running a marketing-led + sales-led hybrid, typical allocation is: 25-35% content and SEO, 15-20% paid search, 15-20% paid social (LinkedIn), 10-15% events and webinars, 5-10% email and nurture, 10-15% tools and technology, 5-10% brand and creative. Allocation should follow your buyer's information habits and your GTM motion.

What is a bottoms-up marketing budget model?

A bottoms-up budget model starts with the revenue goal, works backwards through conversion funnel metrics (close rate, MQL-to-SQL, CPL) to calculate the investment needed to hit the target. It produces a defensible, math-based budget request rather than a percentage-of-revenue benchmark. This is the format that wins budget conversations with CFOs and CEOs.

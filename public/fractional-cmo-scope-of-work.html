Fractional CMO Scope of Work: What to Include and How to Structure It

The scope of work is the most important document in a fractional CMO engagement — and the most commonly done badly. Here's the structure that creates accountability.

By Bill Colbert, founder of Treetop Growth Strategy · Updated June 2026

Most fractional CMO scopes of work are written in one of two failure modes: too vague (just says "provide marketing leadership") or too prescriptive (lists 50 tactical activities with no connection to outcomes). The right scope is output-oriented — it defines what will be produced, by when, and how success will be measured.

The Seven Elements of an Effective Fractional CMO Scope

1. Commitment structure
Hours per week, availability windows, response time expectations, and what happens if the engagement needs to scale up temporarily. Example: "10-15 hours per week, available M-F 9am-5pm CT, 24-hour response time for non-urgent messages, 4-hour response for urgent items."

2. Deliverables by phase
Specific outputs with committed completion dates, not activities. "Deliver a competitive analysis of top 5 competitors by Day 21" is a deliverable. "Perform competitive research" is an activity. See the Fractional CMO Deliverables guide for a complete deliverable list by phase.

3. Functions owned vs functions advised
Clearly define what the fractional CMO owns (decisions they make) vs what they advise on (input they provide, someone else decides). Ambiguity here is the most common source of conflict. Example: "CMO owns: content strategy, agency relationships, marketing budget allocation. CMO advises: product marketing, sales enablement, event strategy."

4. Success metrics
The specific metrics by which the engagement will be evaluated at 90 days, 6 months, and at renewal. Should include both marketing metrics (pipeline contribution %, email performance, organic traffic) and business metrics (revenue from marketing-sourced pipeline). If the fractional CMO refuses to commit to metrics, that's a red flag.

5. Reporting cadence
Weekly async status update, bi-weekly working session with CEO/leadership, monthly metrics review. The format and frequency should be specified so there's no ambiguity about what communication the company is entitled to receive.

6. Team and resource access
What staff does the fractional CMO manage? What budget do they control? What tools and systems do they have access to? What agency or contractor relationships do they manage? A fractional CMO without authority over the resources needed to execute cannot be held accountable for results.

7. Contract terms
Monthly retainer amount, payment terms, notice period for termination (60 days is standard), IP ownership, confidentiality, and any non-compete provisions. Month-to-month with 60-day notice is the most common structure for fractional engagements.

Red Flags in Fractional CMO Scopes

  • No specific deliverables — only activities described
  • No success metrics or the CMO won't commit to any
  • Scope doesn't specify what functions they own vs advise
  • No defined notice period or minimum term
  • Scope requires full-time availability at a fractional price
  • No clarity on which staff or budget the CMO controls

The Scope Conversation

Discuss the scope before the contract. Ask the candidate to draft the first version — how they define the scope tells you a lot about how they think about accountability. A fractional CMO who writes a vague scope is often protecting themselves from being held accountable. A fractional CMO who writes a specific, deliverable-oriented scope is confident in what they can produce.

What should be in a fractional CMO scope of work?

A fractional CMO scope of work should include: commitment structure (hours, availability), deliverables by phase with completion dates, which functions they own vs advise, success metrics for 90-day and 6-month evaluation, reporting cadence, team and budget access, and contract terms including notice period. Scopes that only list activities without deliverables or metrics are the most common cause of failed engagements.

What is a fair fractional CMO contract?

A fair fractional CMO contract includes a monthly retainer reflecting the agreed hours and seniority ($5,000-$25,000/month depending on scope), 60-day termination notice for both parties, clear IP ownership (company owns all work product), confidentiality provisions, and defined deliverables so both parties know what success looks like.

How many hours should a fractional CMO work?

Fractional CMO engagements typically run 10-20 hours per week. Advisory arrangements run 4-8 hours per week. More than 25 hours per week approaches full-time and should be priced and managed accordingly. The right number depends on scope — a CMO managing a team of 5 and an agency needs more hours than one providing strategic direction to a founder-led company.

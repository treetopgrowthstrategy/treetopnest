Marketing Leadership Framework

30-60-90 Day Plan for Marketing Leaders: Benchmarks, Competitive Analysis, and Testing Cadence

Most 30-60-90 day plans tell you what to do. This one tells you what good looks like, how to measure where you are today, and how to run structured tests that produce decisions — not just activity.

By Bill Colbert, founder of Treetop Growth Strategy · Updated June 2026

Most 30-60-90 day marketing plans are written for the hiring manager's comfort, not the marketing leader's effectiveness. They list phases — "listen," "plan," "execute" — without ever answering the questions that actually matter: what does good look like right now? Where does your performance sit relative to the industry? What's the competition doing while you're still finding your footing? And what's the systematic testing process that turns 90 days of activity into 90 days of learning?

This framework is different. It's built around benchmarks first, goals second, and a structured testing cadence throughout. Everything is anchored to numbers — industry averages, good benchmarks, and the competitive signals that tell you where the real leverage is.

Who this is for:

New CMOs, marketing directors, fractional CMOs entering a new engagement, and VPs of Marketing taking over a team. The framework scales from a 3-person startup to a 200-person growth company — adjust the benchmarks to your segment.

Days 1-30

Audit, Benchmark, Competitive Intelligence

Goal: know exactly where you stand and what you're up against before committing to any direction.

The Competitive Intelligence Audit (Days 1-14)

While you're pulling internal numbers, run the competitive audit in parallel. Most new marketing leaders underestimate how much you can learn about competitors from public data in 48 hours. Here's the methodology:

What to audit

  • Competitors' top organic pages (Ahrefs/Semrush)
  • Their paid keyword spend and ad copy
  • Their content velocity (posts/week, new pages/month)
  • Review profiles (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot)
  • LinkedIn follower growth rate + post engagement
  • Job postings (signals investment areas)
  • Backlink acquisition rate (who's linking to them)
  • Positioning language on homepage + pricing page

Questions to answer

  • Where are competitors getting organic traffic we aren't?
  • What keyword clusters do they own that we should?
  • What are customers complaining about in their reviews?
  • What are they spending in paid that we're ignoring?
  • Where are their content gaps (topics they avoid)?
  • What's their primary acquisition channel?
  • Where are they visibly investing headcount?
  • What's their messaging not saying that matters?

Compile this into a one-page competitive matrix before day 15. The document should answer: "What are the top three moves our strongest competitor is making right now, and what's our response?" If you can't answer that by day 15, keep auditing.

Days 1-30 Checklist

  • Baseline metrics table completed and reviewed with CEO/VP Sales
  • CRM data quality audit complete (duplicate rate, field completion, attribution coverage)
  • Competitive matrix built for top 3-5 competitors
  • Keyword gap analysis run (what competitors rank for that we don't)
  • Existing campaign performance review (last 6 months, by channel)
  • Tech stack audit (what's being used, what's redundant, what's missing)
  • Team capacity map (hours available per person per week, current projects)
  • Sales-marketing alignment meeting — agree on MQL definition, SLA, handoff process
  • 30-day hypothesis document written: top 3 things you believe about the marketing situation and how you'll test them in month 2
The Day 30 deliverable:

A written "State of Marketing" document: where you are on each baseline metric vs industry benchmark, what the top 3 competitive threats and opportunities are, and a prioritized list of month 2 bets with specific success criteria. Share this with your CEO. It signals competence, creates alignment, and gives you a document to hold yourself accountable to.

Days 31-60

First Tests, First Bets, Early Data

Goal: run the first structured experiments, generate early signal on what's working, and refine the strategy based on data rather than assumptions.

The Testing Cadence: How to Structure Month 2

Month 2 is when most new marketing leaders make a critical mistake: they ship programs instead of tests. There's an important difference. A program is a commitment to a channel or strategy. A test is a time-bounded experiment with a clear hypothesis, defined success metric, and a pre-committed decision rule for what you'll do based on the result.

The testing cadence for month 2 should have three levels running simultaneously:

Level 1: Messaging Tests (Days 31-45)

Before you invest in any channel, make sure your core message is resonating. Run A/B tests on your homepage headline, primary CTA, and value proposition. These are the highest-leverage tests in your arsenal because a 20% improvement in conversion rate compounds across every channel you're investing in.

Timeline: 2 weeks per test Min sample: 500 visitors per variant Success metric: conversion rate to primary CTA Decision rule: stat sig at 95% confidence

Level 2: Channel Hypothesis Tests (Days 35-60)

Based on your competitive audit, you identified channels where competitors are winning that you're underinvesting in. Run a minimum viable test in each. For paid social: $1,500-$2,000 budget, single audience, single creative, 14-day run. For content: 3 pieces on a new topic cluster, measure organic traction at 30 days. The goal isn't results yet — it's learning the cost of acquisition and engagement in each channel.

Budget: $1,500-$3,000 per channel test Timeline: 14-21 days per test Success metric: CPL and lead quality score Decision rule: continue if CPL within 2x target

Level 3: Sales Enablement Quick Win (Days 31-60)

Find one thing marketing can ship to sales in the next 30 days that will immediately improve their close rate or deal velocity. This is strategic — it creates a marketing-sales win that establishes your credibility with the revenue team faster than any campaign performance will. Common candidates: a competitive battlecard, a case study for the segment they close most, or a demo deck refresh based on objection patterns from the last 90 days of call recordings.

Timeline: 2-3 weeks to produce Success metric: sales team NPS + deal velocity on instrumented deals Decision rule: ship it, then measure usage over 30 days

Days 61-90

Double Down, Measure, Report

Goal: use month 2 test data to make channel and budget allocation decisions, ship the first full programs, and deliver the 90-day report to leadership.

The Decision Framework for Month 3

By day 60, you have data. The month 3 question is: which bets do you double down on, which do you kill, and which need another cycle of testing before you decide? Use this framework:

  • Double down if: cost per lead is within 1.5x target AND lead quality is acceptable AND there's clear room to scale. Increase budget 2-3x and extend for 60 days.
  • Iterate if: the channel showed signal (CPL trending down, engagement strong) but didn't hit target yet. Change one variable (creative, audience, landing page) and run another 21-day cycle.
  • Kill if: CPL is above 3x target after a full cycle with one optimization attempt, or lead quality is consistently poor. Don't run a dead channel for a third month out of sunk cost reasoning.

The 90-Day Report: What Leadership Wants to See

The 90-day report is your most important marketing deliverable. Most new marketing leaders write a summary of what they did. Write a summary of what you learned and what you're committing to. The structure that works:

  1. Where we were: The baseline metrics table from day one, unvarnished.
  2. Where we are: The same metrics at day 90. Show the delta. Don't obscure bad numbers — explain them.
  3. What we learned: The three most important things the tests revealed about your market, your customers, or your competitive position that you didn't know on day one.
  4. What we're betting on: The specific programs you're funding for months 4-6 and why, based on month 2-3 test results.
  5. What we need: Budget, headcount, tool changes, or organizational changes required to execute the bets.

Common Mistakes in the First 90 Days

  • Skipping the baseline: You cannot set meaningful 90-day targets without knowing where you started. "Increase pipeline" is not a goal. "Increase marketing-sourced pipeline from 28% to 35%" is a goal.
  • Committing to programs before you have data: The 30-day audit exists so you can make informed bets, not validate assumptions you had on day one. If your month 2 plan is the same as what you thought you'd do before you started, you didn't audit hard enough.
  • Ignoring competitive intelligence: Your competitive position changes constantly. What your top competitor published last month, what they're ranking for this week, where they're investing paid spend — this should inform your month 2 bets, not be a quarterly exercise.
  • Writing a plan that can't be killed: Every initiative in your 90-day plan should have a pre-defined exit condition. If you can't articulate what result would make you stop investing in something, you're committing to it on faith rather than on data.
  • Under-communicating upward: The 90-day plan is not just a personal roadmap — it's a leadership alignment document. Share the baseline metrics with your CEO on day 5. Share the state of marketing document on day 30. Never let your 90-day report be the first time leadership sees your performance data.

What is a 30-60-90 day plan for a new marketing leader?

A 30-60-90 day marketing plan is a structured framework that defines what a new CMO, marketing director, or VP of Marketing will accomplish in their first three months. The best plans are built around a baseline benchmark audit in days 1-30, structured tests and first programs in days 31-60, and data-driven program investment decisions in days 61-90.

What benchmarks should a new CMO establish in the first 30 days?

The critical benchmarks to pull in your first 30 days include email open and CTR rates (B2B average: 20-25% open, 2-5% CTR), lead-to-MQL conversion (average 10-15%), MQL-to-SQL conversion (average 40-50%), pipeline coverage ratio (minimum 3x quota), marketing-sourced pipeline percentage (average 30-40%), and CAC payback period (average 15-18 months for SaaS).

What should a CMO deliver at the end of the first 90 days?

At day 90, the CMO should deliver: the baseline vs current performance comparison, the top three strategic learnings from month 2 tests, the specific programs being funded for months 4-6 with data-backed rationale, and the resource requirements to execute those bets. A 90-day report that lists activities without showing performance delta is a missed opportunity.

How do you run a competitive audit in your first 30 days?

A first-30-days competitive audit covers: top organic pages and keyword gaps (Ahrefs or Semrush), paid keyword spend and ad copy, content velocity (posts per week), review profile analysis (G2, Capterra), LinkedIn engagement rates, job postings (signals investment priorities), and positioning language comparison. The deliverable is a one-page competitive matrix showing the top three competitor moves and your response.

What is a marketing testing cadence?

A marketing testing cadence is a systematic schedule of structured experiments run in parallel across different marketing layers. In the first 90 days, this typically includes Level 1 (messaging tests: homepage headline and value prop), Level 2 (channel hypothesis tests: minimum viable investment in 2-3 channels with defined success metrics), and Level 3 (sales enablement quick wins). Each test has a pre-defined hypothesis, success metric, and decision rule before it starts.

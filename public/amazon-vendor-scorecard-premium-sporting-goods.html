The Amazon Vendor Scorecard for Premium Sporting Goods Brands

Amazon is not a yes-or-no decision for performance gear brands. It's a strategic evaluation — with eight criteria that determine whether the channel is building your brand or liquidating it. This scorecard is designed for brand operators, fractional CMOs, and investors asking the question that should have been asked sooner: is Amazon actually working for this brand, or are we just hooked on the revenue?

By Bill ColbertTreetop Growth Strategy~12 min read

The Question Most Brands Don't Ask

Every performance gear brand eventually ends up on Amazon. The reasons are always the same: a brand operator notices their product is already being sold there by third-party sellers at undefined margins, decides they should control the listing, and starts selling. Within a year, Amazon represents 30–50% of revenue.

The question brands rarely ask at this point: at what cost?

Amazon revenue is real. But Amazon revenue comes with a set of strategic liabilities that accumulate quietly until they're expensive to reverse. Price visibility that floors your retail partnerships. Brand page limitations that commoditize your story. MAP violations that erode your premium positioning. Customer data you'll never see. And a referral fee + FBA structure that, at premium price points, often leaves less margin than the gym wholesale channel this playbook advocates for.

This scorecard is a thinking tool, not a verdict

The scorecard below is designed to generate a structured conversation, not to tell you to leave Amazon. Most brands should be on Amazon in some capacity. The question is whether your current Amazon posture is strategic or reactive — and whether it's building or eroding the things that make your brand valuable long-term.

The Eight-Criteria Scorecard

Score your brand on each criterion. Each criterion is scored 1–3, where 3 indicates Amazon is working as a strategic asset on that dimension and 1 indicates it's creating a liability. Maximum score: 24. Score interpretation at the bottom.

Gross Margin After Amazon Fees
Amazon typically takes 15% referral fee + 10–20% FBA (pick/pack/storage/returns) = 25–35% channel cost. At a $90 MSRP with 50% COGS, you have $45 gross margin before Amazon takes $27–$32. Score 3 if you're netting 25%+ after all fees; score 1 if you're netting under 15%.

Reading Your Score

20–24
Amazon is a strategic asset — invest and optimize.
You're running Amazon well. The channel is additive, your margins are healthy, you're capturing customer data, and your brand positioning is intact. Continue investing in listing optimization, review generation, and Sponsored Brands. Consider Amazon DSP for retargeting.
14–19
Mixed signal — identify and address the weak criteria.
There are specific liabilities worth fixing before they compound. Prioritize the criteria where you scored 1 or 2. Brand Registry, MAP enforcement, and data capture are often the fastest wins. Don't add channel investment until you've addressed the weak points.
8–13
Amazon is eroding your brand — restructure your posture.
The channel is likely producing revenue while creating compounding liabilities in pricing, positioning, and data. You need a channel restructure: review whether specific product SKUs should be pulled, address MAP violations aggressively, and begin building alternative channels (gym wholesale, DTC growth) before Amazon revenue becomes too concentrated to exit cleanly.
Below 8
Consider exiting Amazon for premium SKUs.
At this score, Amazon is more liability than asset for your premium positioning. Brands like Patagonia have exited Amazon entirely; others use Amazon only for commodity-tier products while protecting premium SKUs for DTC and wholesale. Model what revenue diversification looks like before making the decision — but don't let convenience delay the conversation.

The Relationship Between Amazon Posture and Gym Channel Viability

There's a direct connection between how your brand shows up on Amazon and how attractive you are as a gym wholesale partner. Gym owners will research your brand before agreeing to recommend your product to their members. They'll look at your Amazon listing and make three rapid judgments:

One: Is your product quality reflected in the listing content and reviews? Two: Is the price I'm being offered at wholesale actually below what my members can find on Amazon in 30 seconds? Three: Does this brand feel like the kind of company I want to put my name behind?

A brand with commodity-adjacent Amazon positioning, sub-4.0 reviews, and active MAP violations is not an attractive wholesale partner — regardless of what their product is actually like. The scorecard above is a gym-facing brand audit as much as it is a channel profitability analysis. Fixing your Amazon posture is a prerequisite for building a credible gym wholesale program.

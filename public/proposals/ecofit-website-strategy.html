Product architecture

EcoFit sells two things: analytics and maintenance. Analytics is the entry point — 99.9% of customers come for it first. Maintenance is the module that pays off the full investment. Both are delivered via an always-on portal, with optional white-glove custom reporting studies available as a project engagement. Camera and space analytics are an emerging layer that augments the core IoT data with floor-level utilization visibility.

The buyer and the website's job

The corporate website has one job: when a VP or C-suite operator at a chain like Absolute Recomp searches for EcoFit and lands on the page, they need to know in under 90 seconds that EcoFit is the intelligence layer their facilities need. Existing customers do not use the marketing site — they go straight to the portal. The site is purely for prospective buyers and investors.

The "Living Gym" concept

Tim introduced the defining metaphor for the entire brand: the Living Gym vs. the dead gym. A living gym floor evolves based on real usage data — it shifts equipment, responds to member behavior, and proves new formats before rolling them to franchise locations. A dead gym floor looks the same it did five years ago. EcoFit is the intelligence that makes the living gym possible.

"One's living, one's dead. And to me, it's like, there are three offerings we have here — maximize your equipment, maximize your floor, maximize your members."

— Tim Porth
The cardinal rule — simplicity

Tim's defining insight from his own company: after hiring someone to write the website, the copy was so technical that even Tim couldn't follow it. He rewrote the homepage himself, simplifying it completely. Website sales doubled. This is the governing principle for every page of the EcoFit site: if a VP of Operations can't read it in 90 seconds and know what to do next, rewrite it.

"We were so intelligent that people had to be impressed. And when I read it, I was like — I don't even understand what we're saying."

— Tim Porth
Proof of concept vs. the competition

Phil made a critical point about competitor positioning: many companies in this space build expensive websites claiming they can do things they have never actually done. EcoFit is different — they have the manufacturer API relationships, camera integrations, asset management platform connections, and 5,000+ live installs. The infrastructure is built and running. The website needs to communicate this without sounding like a startup selling a vision.

"All you have to do is say we're in every EOS... 5,000 gyms can't be wrong."

— Phil Rankine
Key strategic decisions made
The website is 100% marketing — no portal documentation, no technical specs
Target buyer is C-suite / VP level, not gym floor managers
"The Living Gym" is the approved brand concept
Three-outcome framework replaces any feature-based structure
5,000+ gyms statistic must appear prominently above the fold
Named partners (EOS, Planet Fitness, Woodway, Matrix, Crew) are non-negotiable credibility anchors
No IoT jargon, no gateway specs, no API descriptions on any customer-facing page
02 — StoryBrand Framework

The BrandScript

Every page of the website flows from this. The framework positions EcoFit as the Guide — not the hero. The C-suite gym operator is the hero. EcoFit helps them win.

1
The character — the hero

VP or C-suite decision maker at a multi-location gym chain

Who they are

Responsible for equipment investments, facility performance, and brand competitiveness across every location. Accountable to a board or franchise network. Makes decisions that cost hundreds of thousands of dollars.

What they want

Fitness facilities that attract and retain members — and the intelligence to keep their clubs ahead of the competition without relying on gut feeling or vendor relationships.

2
External problem

Blind capital decisions

Making six-figure equipment and facility decisions without knowing what is actually being used, what is breaking down, or what members want.

2
Internal problem

Fear of falling behind

The unsettling sense that competitors are making smarter moves — and that your clubs are slipping without you knowing it.

2

Operators deserve better

A gym operator investing millions in their facilities should not have to rely on a salesperson's opinion. Real data should drive real decisions.

3

EcoFit understands the problem firsthand

03 — Page-by-Page Recommendations

Website structure

Five pages. Every recommendation below maps directly to what came out of the braintrust session. Click each page to expand the full guidance.

Page 1 Homepage
+

Hero section

Lead with an outcome-first headline anchored to "The Living Gym." Example direction: "Your gym is alive. Are you listening to it?" No product names, no feature descriptions, no technology references in the hero.

Hero image or visual should depict a modern, high-energy gym in motion — not a dashboard screenshot or hardware close-up. The emotion comes first, the product proof comes second.

Immediately below the hero — social proof stats bar

This is EcoFit's most underleveraged asset. A stats strip showing: 5,000+ Gyms | Equipment Pieces Connected | Workouts Tracked Daily | Manufacturing Partnerships. Phil's framing is the vibe: "5,000 gyms can't be wrong."

Logo strip directly below: EOS, Planet Fitness, Woodway, Matrix, Crew. A C-suite buyer who recognizes these names stops scrolling.

Three-outcome framework section

Maximize Your Equipment — extend life, eliminate unplanned downtime, pay off the investment

Maximize Your Floor — optimize space and mix based on real usage, not gut feeling

Maximize Your Members — member-level insight and experience (positioned as forward-looking)

Analogy section

Use Tim's Whoop Band framing: "Think of EcoFit as a Whoop Band for your gym — deep analytics that tell you not just what happened, but what to do about it." Pair with a dashboard or heat map visual.

Remove cut

Any section explaining how the technology works. Cameras, gateways, API feeds, IoT devices — none of it belongs on the homepage. Phil said it directly: "If we dive into technical trackers and gateways, people are just going to glaze over."

Page 2 Platform / Product
+

Lead with the two core pillars — in the right sequence

Analytics first (Dave confirmed 99.9% of customers come for analytics). Maintenance second — but frame it as "the module that pays for the whole investment," not a utilitarian add-on.

Three tiers of analytics delivery

Portal Analytics — out-of-the-box, self-serve, always on

Custom Reporting — white-glove data projects, formal SOW engagements

AI-Powered Insights — near-future capability, signals roadmap without overpromising

Heat map visual

Phil's floor heat mapping example — PDF of the facility, color-coded by traffic and usage over time — is the single most compelling product story in the braintrust session. Even a mockup version will do more work than any copy block.

"Proven, not promised" callout

Competitors build flashy websites claiming things they have never done. EcoFit has the manufacturer API relationships, camera partnerships, and asset management integrations — all live. A dedicated callout: "We didn't design a concept. We built the infrastructure — and it's already running in 5,000 gyms."

Remove cut

Hardware photography that makes EcoFit look like an installation project rather than an intelligence platform. Any section positioning hardware as the product.

Page 3 Solutions / Who We Serve
+

Reorganize around three buyer types — not use cases

Corporate Gym Chains: The C-suite buyer at a multi-location group. They want comparable data across properties, trend insight before it hits their market, and proof they can hand to franchisees. Tim's "fast follower" concept is the value prop — see what's working in California before it reaches your market.

Franchisees: Needs data to justify decisions to corporate — and to justify new layouts to their own ops team. Phil and Tim both landed on this: prove the model in two clubs, refine it, roll it to fifty. Confidence is the product.

OEM / Manufacturing Partners: Woodway, Matrix, Crew. They license EcoFit under their own brand. This is a completely different buyer — they're embedding EcoFit as a value-add in their equipment offering. This persona may warrant its own sub-section with partner-specific copy and a different CTA.

Page 4 About
+

Lead with infrastructure, not origin story

The About page should open with the network EcoFit has built, not a founding timeline. Phil's line is the headline candidate: "We have the relationships with the manufacturers for the API feeds. We have the camera relationships. We have the tie-ins to the asset management firms. We're not playing lip service."

Stats anchor this page

An investor or skeptical VP who lands here should see scale immediately — installs, connections, partnerships — before they read a single team bio.

Team bios are brief and secondary

Dave, Phil, Tim — credible operators with deep industry experience. Team bios support the story and reinforce trust, but they follow the proof of scale, not precede it.

Global Navigation & Global Elements
+

Add "Sign In" to the navigation — a subtle but important signal that there is a real product behind the marketing site.

CTA language: move away from "Request a Demo." Use "Talk to an EcoFit Analyst" (primary) and "See How It Works" (secondary).

No technology jargon in nav labels. "Platform" works. "IoT Solutions" or "Connectivity" does not.

The universal test for every page: can a VP of Operations at a mid-size gym chain read this in 90 seconds and know exactly what they would get? If a section requires a second read, rewrite it.

05 — Governing Principles

The rules that govern every page

These emerged directly from the April strategy session and reflect the team's own thinking. They serve as the filter for every design and copy decision going forward.

01
EcoFit is the Guide, not the Hero
The C-suite gym operator is the hero of this story. EcoFit is the experienced guide who hands them the tools to win. Every headline, section, and CTA should reinforce this. The moment the website starts celebrating EcoFit's technology for its own sake, it has failed.
02
The 90-second clarity test
Tim shared a defining experience from his other company: after investing in professional copywriting, the website was so technically written that even he could not follow it. He simplified the homepage himself, and sales doubled. Every page of the EcoFit site passes this test: a VP of Operations at a mid-size gym chain reads it cold and immediately knows what they would get, why it matters, and what to do next. If a section requires a second read, it gets rewritten.
03
Proven, not promised
Competitors build expensive websites claiming they can do things they have never done. EcoFit has done it. Every page should communicate this distinction — 5,000 live installs, named partners, built infrastructure — without saying "we're better than the competition." Show the scale. Let the buyer draw the conclusion.
04
Lead with outcomes, not mechanisms
No IoT jargon. No gateways. No API descriptions. No camera specs. The Living Gym, Maximize Your Floor, actionable insights — these are the words. The mechanisms that produce them are for the sales call, the demo, and the implementation team. They do not belong on the marketing site.
05
5,000 gyms is a headline, not a footnote
This number is currently buried or absent. It should be one of the first things a visitor sees. Alongside the named partner logos, it answers the buyer's biggest question — "can I trust these people?" — before they have even read the first paragraph. Lead with the proof.

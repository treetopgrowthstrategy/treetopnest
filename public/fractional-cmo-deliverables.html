Fractional CMO Deliverables: What You Should Actually Receive

The most expensive fractional CMO mistake is a vague scope of work. Here's what a high-performing engagement produces, month by month, and how to hold your CMO to it.

By Bill Colbert, founder of Treetop Growth Strategy · Updated June 2026

Fractional CMO engagements fail in a predictable pattern: the scope is defined in terms of activities ("lead our marketing strategy," "manage the team") rather than deliverables. Activities are hard to evaluate and easy to claim credit for. A fractional CMO who "led the content strategy" for three months may have attended meetings about content strategy — or may have produced a complete content architecture, editorial calendar, and three months of published pieces. Both are "leading content strategy." Only one is worth paying for.

Use this document as a scope of work addendum or an evaluation framework before signing an engagement.

Month 1 Deliverables (Days 1-30)

  • Marketing Audit Report: A written assessment of current marketing performance across every active channel. Includes traffic by source, email metrics, pipeline attribution, content performance, paid media efficiency, and tech stack review. Should include actual numbers, not impressions. Length: 10-15 pages minimum.
  • Competitive Analysis: Assessment of top 3-5 competitors covering organic search position, content strategy, paid channels, positioning language, and review sentiment. Should identify the top three competitive opportunities to exploit.
  • ICP Refinement: Updated ideal customer profile document with firmographic, technographic, and behavioral criteria — reviewed and agreed upon by sales leadership.
  • 30-Day Priority List: The top five marketing interventions ranked by impact and effort, with owners and committed timelines. This is the roadmap month 2 executes against.
  • Baseline Metrics Dashboard: A live dashboard (Google Sheets, Looker, or native tool) showing all key marketing metrics — set up so leadership can see current performance without asking for updates.

Month 2 Deliverables (Days 31-60)

  • Positioning and Messaging Architecture: The company's core positioning statement, differentiated value propositions by segment, and the messaging hierarchy that flows from them. This document should be used by sales, content, and paid media as the source of truth for what to say and how to say it.
  • Content Strategy: A documented content plan with pillar topics, supporting cluster pages, editorial calendar for the next 90 days, and distribution strategy. Not a list of blog post ideas — a complete architecture tied to keyword research and pipeline goals.
  • Demand Gen Program Launch: At least one demand gen program actively running with defined success metrics. Could be a content cluster, a paid campaign, an event, or a partner program — but something is in market and being measured.
  • Sales Enablement Package: At least one sales enablement asset produced and in use by the sales team: a competitive battlecard, a case study, an updated demo deck, or an objection handling guide.

Month 3 Deliverables (Days 61-90)

  • 90-Day Marketing Report: Baseline vs current on every metric from the day-one audit. What moved, what didn't, and the honest explanation for both. Presented to the CEO or board.
  • Marketing-to-Pipeline Attribution: A documented model showing how marketing activities connect to pipeline and closed revenue. Not perfect attribution (it doesn't exist) but a defensible methodology your CEO and VP Sales can agree on.
  • Team Assessment and Recommendations: Written assessment of the current marketing team's capacity, capabilities, and gaps — with hiring or agency recommendations as warranted.
  • Months 4-6 Plan: The committed marketing investment plan for the next phase with specific programs, budgets, and success metrics. The fractional CMO's ongoing engagement should be scoped against this plan.

What a Fractional CMO Should NOT Be Doing

If your fractional CMO is spending significant time on these, you have a scope or capability problem:

  • Writing every piece of content personally (they should direct and review, not draft)
  • Managing social media day-to-day
  • Designing graphics or building web pages
  • Running individual paid campaigns without strategic direction established
  • Attending every sales call to provide marketing context (once is fine; consistently is not)

A fractional CMO who is primarily doing individual contributor work is either over-scoped, under-resourced (no team to execute), or not operating at the right level. Name the problem and fix it in month two, not month six.

What should a fractional CMO deliver in the first month?

In the first 30 days, a fractional CMO should deliver: a marketing audit report with actual performance data across all channels; a competitive analysis of top 3-5 competitors; an updated ICP document agreed upon by sales; a prioritized action list for month 2; and a live baseline metrics dashboard. If you don't have these by day 30, you're behind.

How do you measure fractional CMO performance?

Measure against specific deliverables with committed timelines (not activity descriptions), and against marketing metrics at 60 and 90 days. Key metrics: marketing-sourced pipeline percentage, email performance, organic traffic growth, and content production volume. A fractional CMO who can't show a written marketing audit by day 30 is not performing.

What is a fractional CMO scope of work?

A fractional CMO scope of work should define: weekly hours and availability, specific deliverables by month (not just activities), which functions they own vs advise, reporting cadence and format, success metrics for the engagement, and contract terms including notice period. Scopes defined only in terms of activities ('lead marketing strategy') are the most common source of fractional CMO disappointment.

How long should a fractional CMO engagement last?

Most fractional CMO engagements run 6-18 months. The first 90 days are the foundation period. Months 4-12 are where results accumulate. The engagement ends when either the company hires a full-time CMO, the company decides not to prioritize marketing leadership, or the fractional CMO has accomplished the specific objectives they were brought in for.

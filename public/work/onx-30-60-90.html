01
Discipline mix
Striking-heavy or MMA-blended. BJJ-only schools are deprioritized. The gear catalog is gloves, headgear, shin guards, hand wraps. Pure grappling schools do not consume that gear at scale.

Membership-based with progression curriculum. Drop-in studios and class-pass models are deprioritized. Recurring members generate the replacement cycle that funds the channel.

Affiliated with a recognized lineage, fighter, or method — ATT, Tiger Schulmann, Gracie Barra, 10th Planet. Brand-name gyms make brand-name gear partnerships work.

Five revenue motions inside a partner gym
Signing the gym is not enough. Gyms forget to sell. The activation playbook converts the partnership into recurring revenue through five member-moments each year. Hayabusa and Venum can sell gloves to a gym. They cannot send a UFC coach to run a seminar at the gym. The hardest-to-replicate offering in the competitive set is the ONX founder relationship.
01
Welcome kit · Onboarding moment
Every new member receives an ONX welcome kit (hand wraps, basic gear) in their first month. Gym buys at wholesale, builds into membership pricing or sells as add-on. Predictable unit per new member acquired.
02
Sparring bundle · Progression trigger
When a member moves from cardio to sparring class, headgear and shin guards become mandatory. Gym presents the "Sparring-Ready Bundle" at ~$400. A real merchandising moment tied to a natural milestone.
03
Quarterly refresh · Replacement cycle
Gloves wear in 6–12 months. Structured refresh promotion: trade in old gloves for $50 off new ones. Repeatable. Predictable. Gives the gym a sales conversation hook every quarter.
04
Sponsored fighter · Fight-night moment
The gym's amateurs and pros wear ONX at every local card. Photographed, broadcast, posted on gym social. Built-in replacement cycle from fight wear and a content asset at every event.
05
Wittman seminar · Annual event
Once per year per top partner. Half-day UFC-connected coach seminar at the gym. Members pay $150 to attend. Gear sells at the event. Gym keeps event revenue. ONX owns the brand moment.

Deal term framework — questions to resolve in month 1
TermStructureRationale
Annual placement fee ONX pays the gym a monthly flat fee ($200–$500/location depending on tier), OR negotiates a rev share override in lieu of a flat fee. Fee covers signage real estate and access to the new member sales floor. The gym is providing shelf space and a captive audience. They need a reason to prioritize ONX over the next brand that walks in. A flat fee creates an obligation; rev share aligns incentives.
Staff product allocation Onboarding: 2–4 hero SKUs (gloves + one apparel piece) at cost or free for head coach/GM. Annual credit of $300–$500/location for ongoing staff gear. Treated as a marketing cost, not COGS. The coach wearing and using the product visibly is the highest-leverage endorsement in the building. Every class is a product demo.
On-site inventory Small curated display: 2–3 hero SKUs (gloves, hand wraps) at wholesale. Gym owns the inventory and the markup on in-person sales. Full catalog available via QR to DTC. Hybrid model, not binary. Impulse buys happen at the gym. LTV and catalog depth happen on the DTC channel. Both matter. Avoid forcing the gym to hold deep inventory — keep the wholesale risk low to lower the barrier to entry.
Wholesale + markup model T1 networks: 40–50% wholesale margin. T2 flagships: 50–60% wholesale margin. MAP enforcement required in the agreement — gym cannot discount below MSRP without approval. MAP protection is non-negotiable. One gym discounting heavily against MSRP undercuts the brand value for every other partner and for the DTC channel simultaneously.
New member pass-through Yes — this is the Welcome Kit mechanic. Gym buys the kit at wholesale and either builds it into membership pricing or sells it as an add-on at their own markup. ONX still captures the member email via QR even when the transaction happens at gym POS. Clean and margin-positive for the gym. Drives the attachment rate metric that funds the model. The QR path preserves the ONX customer relationship even when the gym owns the transaction.
Financing for large orders Net-30 terms for T1 network initial buys across 10+ locations. Consignment on first buy for T2 flagship to reduce capital risk before sell-through is proven. No formal credit facility at this stage. Consignment removes the hesitation for elite flagships who are used to being courted, not invoiced. Net-30 is industry-standard for wholesale. Full upfront payment is a deal-killer at this stage.
In-club signage ONX provides creative assets and covers print production cost. Gym provides approved placement locations per the agreement. Standardized formats (banner sizes, vinyl specs) — no custom creative per gym. QR codes embedded in all signage. Standardization keeps production costs predictable and prevents the signage program from becoming a custom design job for every partner. QR-embedded signage converts brand awareness into trackable DTC revenue.
QR code for ecommerce Each gym gets a unique QR that routes to a co-branded ONX landing page with their discount code pre-applied. Tracks attribution. Captures email at checkout. Feeds the practitioner email sequence. Can be embedded in signage, printed on the gear guide, and displayed at the front desk. The QR is the bridge between in-gym awareness and owned-channel revenue. It lets ONX maintain the DTC data relationship even when the gym is the physical touchpoint — and it makes every piece of signage a measurable revenue driver.

Key insight from benchmark
Sanabul is the cautionary tale — Amazon-native, 20,000+ reviews, built entirely on volume at $30–$60 gloves. ONX cannot out-review them at launch. The play is to own the premium practitioner segment ($150–$350) where Hayabusa and Venum dominate but the review gap is smaller and the buyer is less price-sensitive. A generalist agency will race to the bottom on price to drive volume. That destroys the brand before it has a chance to build.

→
Review velocity analysis
For the top 3 competitors per category, estimate how many reviews they're generating monthly. A new ASIN with 10 reviews against a competitor at 8,000 is invisible. The operator needs a clear review acquisition strategy and the patience to build. Set the expectation with Desmond before selecting a vendor.

Pricing + margin floor analysis
Map competitor pricing against ONX's current MSRP. Factor in: Amazon referral fee (~15%), FBA fulfillment cost, storage fees, and the operator revenue share (3–30%). If the contribution margin math doesn't work at ONX's price floor, that needs to surface before selecting a vendor — not after.

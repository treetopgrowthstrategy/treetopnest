Pillar One
Merch Injection Into the Existing DTC Customer LTV Flow

The gear customer is already bought in. They trusted the brand enough to spend $70 to $90 on a rash guard or fight shorts. That trust is the highest-cost thing in consumer commerce to earn, and most performance gear brands spend it exactly once: at the original gear purchase.

Merch injection is the practice of weaving performance apparel into every touch point where the gear customer already exists. Not as a separate campaign, but as a natural extension of the gear journey. A customer who bought a rash guard is one email away from knowing why the matching performance tee belongs in the same bag. That email costs almost nothing. The trust that makes it work was already earned at the first purchase.

Bundle Strategy

The highest-leverage DTC move is a named bundle that pairs gear and apparel at a slight discount. Not a generic "complete your kit" suggestion, but a specifically named product that has its own page, its own story, and its own hero image.

Two bundles cover 80 percent of the opportunity:

A
The Precision Bundle (gear-forward)
A rash guard and fight shorts paired with a performance tee. The gear is the anchor; the tee is the merch introduction. Price the bundle at 12 to 15 percent off the sum of individual items. The tee earns the discount while converting a gear buyer into a merch buyer. Every subsequent purchase of apparel alone is pure margin expansion because the acquisition cost was paid at the gear purchase.
Best for gear-first DTC traffic
B
The Athlete Kit (name it after the endorser)
A full training kit bundled under the athlete's name: the exact rash guard, shorts, and tee the athlete trains in. The named athlete context converts at higher rates than a generic bundle because the buyer is purchasing identity alongside gear. Price at 10 percent off the sum. The discount is justified by the bundle page production value (hero video from training content, athlete quote, specific fit notes from the athlete on each piece).
Best for athlete-driven traffic

Both bundles should have standalone product pages, not just cart logic. A bundle with a URL and a hero image is discoverable via search and shareable as a gift recommendation. Cart logic alone captures only active buyers; a product page captures intent at the research stage.

In-Cart Upsells

The cart is the highest-intent moment in the purchase flow. A customer with a rash guard in the cart is already in purchase mode. Adding an upsell for a performance tee or branded hat at this moment costs nothing in acquisition and converts at 15 to 25 percent in similar performance categories.

The right mechanics:

1
Gear-triggered apparel upsell
When a customer adds any gear SKU (rash guard, fight shorts, compression shorts) to cart, surface a single apparel upsell below the cart summary. One product, one click. Not a carousel. The framing: "Most fighters pair this with the [Performance Tee] for training days when they're not grappling." The specificity of "most fighters" framing lifts conversion versus generic "you might also like" copy.
2
Hat as the micro-upsell
Branded hats priced at $20 to $30 function as an impulse add when the cart already contains $70 to $90 in gear. The framing is pure convenience: "Add the [Brand] cap to your order for $24." No story needed. At sub-$30 pricing, the decision takes three seconds. This is the highest-volume low-friction upsell available to a performance gear brand.

Post-Purchase Email Architecture

The post-purchase sequence for a gear buyer should do three things over the first 30 days: make the original purchase feel smart, introduce the apparel line in context, and create a reason to return. Most brands do the first thing and skip the other two.

Day 0
Order confirmation (standard, high open rate)
Include one line in the confirmation email that plants the merch seed: "Most fighters who train in this rash guard add the [Performance Tee] to their kit within the first month." Link to the tee. No aggressive CTA. This is positioning, not selling. Open rate on order confirmations is 60 to 80 percent, making it the highest-distribution real estate in the entire email channel.
Day 3
Care and usage content
A short email on how to care for technical performance fabric: cold wash, air dry, no fabric softener. This email reduces returns, extends product life, and signals that the brand thinks past the sale. It also creates a natural hook to introduce the apparel line: "When you're not on the mat, the [Performance Tee] is built from the same antimicrobial fabric so you can train twice a day and stay fresh." One-click add to cart from the email.
Day 7
How-to-layer content
A "how fighters layer their kit" email showing the complete training outfit: rash guard under compression shorts, performance tee for between rounds, hat for the walk out. This email is educational, not sales-y, but it shows the full wardrobe and links every piece. The athlete training content is the hero asset here. A 30-second clip of the endorser demonstrating the layering does more than any copy.
Day 14
Size and fit follow-up
Check in on fit. "How is the rash guard fitting?" with a link to the size guide and a clear, low-friction exchange process. Reducing return friction increases lifetime value because customers who successfully exchange once buy again. This email also creates the opening to introduce the apparel size guide side by side: "The performance tees run true to size if the rash guard was right for you."
Day 30
Drop preview or reorder nudge
For brands that do seasonal drops: a preview of the upcoming colorway with an early-access link. For evergreen SKUs: a "your training partner just picked this up" social proof email featuring the tee or a new apparel SKU. The 30-day mark is when the gear has been used enough that buying more from the brand is a natural next step. Meet the customer here.
The segment that matters most

Tag every gear buyer in your email platform as a "gear purchaser" segment on purchase. This segment has proven purchase intent, lower CAC on reengagement, and higher email open rates than the cold subscriber list. All merch email flows should prioritize this segment above any other list for the first campaign.

Athlete Content as a Merch Channel

The endorser athlete is already creating content. Training footage, pre-fight prep, post-camp recovery, technique breakdowns. In almost every case, the athlete is wearing the brand's gear or apparel in that content without any product tagging or link structure to capture the viewer intent that content generates.

Fixing this is a strategy conversation with the athlete's team and the social media manager, not a production conversation. The content already exists. What needs to change is the tagging architecture.

1
Tag specific SKUs in training content
Every training video where the athlete is visibly wearing a rash guard, shorts, or tee should have that exact SKU tagged in the caption or as a product link on platforms that support it. Not a general "shop link" to the homepage. The exact product. A viewer who pauses to look at the shorts the athlete is wearing in round three wants to buy those shorts, not browse a catalog.
Highest-leverage, lowest-cost
2
Build the named athlete bundle page
Create a dedicated landing page: "Train Like [Athlete Name]." The page features the training kit the athlete actually uses, with a hero image from real training content and a quote from the athlete about why they train in specific pieces. The bundle is the three-item gear-plus-tee kit at a slight discount. This page becomes the conversion destination for all athlete content tagging and the link-in-bio target.
Drives highest-AOV conversion
3
Studio content shot list
When the athlete is in a studio session, build a dedicated shot list for merch: solo shots in the performance tee wearing the brand hat, with no competing gear in frame. These are the lifestyle assets the brand needs for apparel PDPs, email headers, and ad creative. Production cost is zero beyond the session time already scheduled. Returns are months of social content and PDP imagery.
Requires session coordination

Sizing Content to Reduce Return Friction

Performance apparel in combat sports has a specific sizing problem that generic apparel brands don't have: the customer is buying for athletic fit at a weight class, not a standard body type. A 185-pound welterweight and a 185-pound recreational gym-goer have completely different body compositions and different sizing needs in compression fit apparel.

Brands that solve this with a one-size-up recommendation and a detailed size-by-product guide reduce returns by 20 to 30 percent in this category. A size guide that says "if you're between sizes in fight shorts, go up; if you're between sizes in compression, stay true" converts uncertainty into a confident purchase.

The format that works best is a two-column guide: product name on the left, fit notes and size-up guidance on the right. Linked from every product page and surfaced in the Day 14 follow-up email. It requires 30 minutes to write and saves weeks of return processing.

Pillar Two
The Gym GTM Motion

The gym channel is the only distribution model that puts a performance gear brand in front of 200 to 500 qualified buyers at once, through a trusted authority (the head coach), in the exact context where the product would be used. No ad campaign replicates that. No influencer has that level of purchase authority over their audience.

The reason most performance gear brands have not cracked this channel is not that gyms don't want the product. It is that brands approach gyms with a vendor pitch when gyms are looking for a partner pitch. The gym owner who has been pitched by 15 apparel companies is not impressed by a wholesale catalog. They want to know what the program does for their gym's identity, their coaches' credibility, and their members' experience.

The Fight Team Program: Reframing the Pitch

The single most important tactical reframe in the gym GTM motion is this: do not pitch co-branded apparel. Pitch a 12-month Fight Team Program.

The reframe in plain language

Instead of: "We'd like to sell your gym co-branded rash guards."

Say: "We'd like to make your gym the official Fight Team partner for [Brand] in your market. That means four seasonal gear drops per year, your name on the gear your fighters train in, and a member discount code so your community can buy direct. Twelve-month partnership, structured quarterly."

This reframe matters for three reasons. First, it shifts the buying decision from "can we afford inventory?" to "do we want our gym to be a Fight Team partner?" Those are different questions. The second question has a tribal answer that runs deeper than cash flow. Second, a 12-month program billed quarterly aligns the gym's cash payments with the season drops they receive, so the payment schedule feels like a natural exchange rather than an upfront cost. Third, the annual contract structure means the brand gets predictable revenue and renewal rate data rather than one-time wholesale orders that have no retention mechanic.

Three-Tier Deal Structure

Tier 1
Pilot Partner
$5K to $10K
Single-location gym. First-year relationship. 200 to 400 units across 2 seasonal drops. Structured as 30/30/40 milestone payments (signing, delivery, net-60). Ideal for gyms with 150 to 300 members where the coach has strong identity authority. Win condition: coach wears the gear on the floor every class.
Tier 2
Growth Partner
$13K to $25K
One to three locations. Annual Fight Team Program with quarterly drops. Billing matched to drop schedule. 700 to 900 units annually. Gym has established identity and member purchase behavior. May include a DTC co-branded landing page (Option A) for member ordering with 15 to 20% revenue share back to gym.
Tier 3
Scale Partner
$30K+
Multi-location operator or affiliate network. Annual program with dedicated seasonal calendar. Co-branding on training room signage and member welcome kits. DTC Option A landing pages for each location. At this tier, financing terms (Affirm B2B or Balance 12-month) are a deal requirement, not an option. These gyms evaluate the brand like a vendor evaluation.

Option A vs Option B: Fulfillment Architecture

Every gym wholesale deal requires a fulfillment decision that shapes both unit economics and data ownership. Get this choice right early because switching mid-program is disruptive to both the gym and the brand's operations.

Option A is the right default for year one. It removes the biggest objection (inventory risk) and captures the most valuable long-term asset (member email data). A gym whose members are in your Klaviyo list becomes a compounding asset. A gym that bought wholesale inventory is a one-time transaction.

Reserve Option B for established Tier 2 and Tier 3 gyms that have demonstrated sell-through history, want full margin control, and are large enough to turn inventory reliably. Option B for a Tier 1 pilot gym is the fastest way to end a new relationship: if the gym can't sell through what they ordered, the program fails and they blame the brand.

Entry Mechanics and Warm Intro Sequence

The outreach sequence for the gym channel follows a specific order. Cold outreach to gym owners closes at under 5 percent in any category. Warm introductions from endorser athletes to their home gyms or affiliated training camps close at 30 to 50 percent. Sequence matters more than volume.

1
Coach's Kit as the entry point
Before any commercial pitch, send the head coach a full fit kit at no charge: one of each size in the primary gear and apparel SKUs. The coach tries the product, forms a fit opinion, and begins wearing the brand on the floor. This is not a sponsorship. It is a product trial that creates social proof where the deal will eventually close. A coach who has been wearing a rash guard for three weeks before the pitch meeting is not evaluating the brand. They are already in the relationship.
Use for every Tier 1 and 2 outreach
2
Athlete warm intro
The endorser athlete likely trains at a specific gym and has training relationships with coaches at other gyms in their market. A text or DM from the athlete to the gym owner carries more weight than any sales outreach. The athlete says: "My gear brand is building a Fight Team Program. I thought of you first. Worth a conversation?" The gym owner says yes. The commercial conversation is now a warm handoff rather than a cold pitch.
Highest close rate mechanism available
3
Pre-order mechanic for Tier 1 close
Once the gym is in conversation, offer a pre-order structure that removes their cash-flow objection before it surfaces. The gym runs a 14-day pre-order window among members. Members pay 50 percent at pre-order, 50 percent at delivery. The gym uses member deposits to fund the brand's deposit. Zero brand credit risk. Zero gym cash-flow friction. The gym's own members finance the deal. This is the highest-leverage operational design in the Tier 1 motion.
Eliminates cash-flow objection entirely

Pillar Three
B2B Financing: Closing the Deals That Almost Happen

The gym GTM motion generates a predictable objection: cash flow. The gym wants the program. The coach is wearing the gear. The close is there. Then the gym owner looks at a $9,900 invoice due in 30 days and hesitates. Financing is the mechanic that converts hesitation into signature.

The right structure depends on deal size. Tier 1 deals rarely need an external financing partner. The milestone payment structure (30 percent at signing, 30 percent at delivery, 40 percent at net-60) is enough to close most hesitations at the Tier 1 level. Tier 2 and Tier 3 deals benefit from formal B2B BNPL partners who pay the brand in full at delivery while the gym repays over 12 months.

Why absorbing the financing fee makes sense in year one
B2B BNPL fee at 3% on a $9,900 deal $297
Close rate without financing terms 20%
Close rate with financing (conservative estimate) 28%
Expected revenue gain per pitch from the close rate lift $792
Net gain after absorbing the fee $495 per pitch

The framing matters as much as the structure. Financing is not a concession. It is a positioning signal. A gear brand that offers payment-term flexibility is telling the gym owner: "We believe in your sell-through enough to take deferred payment." That confidence is the same message the Fight Team Program sends. They reinforce each other.

When a Tier 3 gym is evaluating the brand against a more established competitor, financing terms can be the deciding factor at price parity. The competitor asks for a check. The brand makes the deal work with the gym's cash flow. That is a different conversation. The brand wins it.

Putting the Three Pillars Together

The three pillars are not competing for the same revenue. The DTC LTV injection monetizes the existing gear customer base. The gym GTM motion opens a new distribution channel that reaches buyers the DTC channel cannot. The financing structures make the gym channel deals close at a rate that justifies the outreach investment.

They also reinforce each other in ways that compound over time. Gym partners drive members to the co-branded DTC pages, those members enter the post-purchase email flow, the email flow introduces the full apparel line, and some percentage of gym members become direct brand customers who then refer others. The gym channel is not just a revenue line. It is a customer acquisition channel with a very different unit economics profile than paid social.

The brand that builds all three mechanics in sequence over 12 months is not managing three separate programs. It is running one coherent merch revenue architecture where each pillar feeds the others.

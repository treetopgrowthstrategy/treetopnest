Why Combat Sports Gear Brands Are Losing the Gym Channel

Hayabusa, Venum, and Everlast collectively represent hundreds of millions of dollars in annual combat sports gear revenue. None of them run a structured gym partnership program. This is either a strategic oversight or a deliberate constraint — and either way, it's the most significant first-mover opportunity in the space.

By Bill ColbertTreetop Growth Strategy~10 min read

The Constraint That Created the Opportunity

The three biggest combat sports gear brands — Hayabusa, Venum, and Everlast — each arrived at the same distribution architecture through different paths. But the architecture is the same: DTC online, Amazon, and sporting goods retail. Gyms are an afterthought at best, treated as casual wholesale accounts rather than strategic distribution infrastructure.

This isn't an accident. It's a structural constraint. Here's why each of the major brands is stuck in the channel they're in.

Hayabusa
Premium · DTC-first
Hayabusa is the highest-positioned brand in the combat sports gear category — their gloves retail from $120 to $200+, and their brand equity is built on elite-level fighters and technical product differentiation. The gym channel presents a pricing problem: at 40–45% off MSRP, they're selling $120 gloves at $66–$72. At volume, this creates channel conflict with their DTC margin. Their current solution is to ignore the gym channel entirely and capture reorders through organic brand preference. The gap: gym owners who love Hayabusa quality have no structured way to recommend it to their members.
Gym Program
None
Venum
UFC-licensed · Global retail
Venum's acquisition by Authentic Brands Group and their UFC partnership fundamentally changed their distribution focus. They're a licensing and retail brand now — their revenue comes from UFC merchandise rights, not from building a field sales team to call on independent boxing gyms. Venum has essentially exited the independent gym channel by going upmarket into global retail. Independent gym owners can find Venum product on Amazon, but there's no wholesale relationship to build.
Gym Program
Effectively none
Everlast
Mass-market · Retail-first
Everlast is the legacy brand — distributed through Walmart, Target, Dick's Sporting Goods, and Amazon at price points ($25–$75 for gloves) that make wholesale to gyms structurally problematic. Their margins at mass retail are compressed enough that building a specialized gym wholesale program would require a separate product line or a separate brand. They've chosen neither. Everlast occupies the low end of the market; the mid-to-premium gym partner opportunity sits between Everlast and Hayabusa's price points.
Gym Program
Ad hoc only

What "No Gym Program" Actually Means in the Market

When the three dominant brands in your category have no structured gym distribution, one of two things is true: either the gym channel isn't worth pursuing, or nobody has built it yet. The data argues strongly for the second.

Consider the purchase triggers in a boxing gym environment: a new member needs gloves, a coach recommends a specific pair in class, a training partner's gear gets noticed after a round. These are all conversion events that happen in the gym, not at a keyboard. The brand whose product is part of that moment — physically present, coach-endorsed, built into the welcome kit — captures the sale. The brand whose product is only findable on Amazon captures it only if the member happens to remember the name later.

The search signal confirms the gap

"Wholesale boxing gloves" — 150 searches/month, KD 0. "Wholesale boxing equipment" — 90/month, KD 9. These are purchasing-intent queries from gym owners actively looking for wholesale relationships. The major brands have no content, no channel, and no presence for these searches. The ranking floor is essentially zero.

The First-Mover Advantage in the Gym Channel

Gym partnerships are sticky. Once a brand becomes the "official gear of [Gym Name]" — meaning the brand in the welcome kit, the gear the coaches wear, the product in the QR scan — they're embedded in the gym's member experience. Replacing them requires a new welcome kit design, new signage, a new QR code setup, and a conversation with members who've been receiving that brand since they joined.

This switching cost is the first-mover's primary asset. The brand that activates 200 gyms before any major player notices the channel has a two-to-three-year head start on building those embedded relationships — and the case studies, testimonials, and coach endorsements that make closing the next 200 gyms faster and cheaper.

What the Whitespace Opportunity Actually Looks Like

The gym channel opportunity isn't a mass-market play. It's a precision play for brands in a specific price range and positioning window.

01
The Target Price Range
$65–$130 MSRP for gloves and core equipment. Above Everlast's commodity tier. Below Hayabusa's premium positioning. This is where gym owners feel comfortable recommending to members without the product feeling aspirational or inaccessible. It's also the range where wholesale economics work sustainably at 40% off.
02
The Target Gym Type
Independent boxing gyms, MMA academies, Muay Thai schools, and combat-focused fitness concepts. NOT big-box gyms, UFC Gym franchises (already locked into corporate supply agreements), or general fitness facilities that offer boxing as a class format.
03
The Target Geography
Start with markets where the independent boxing and MMA gym density is highest: Los Angeles, Chicago, New York, Miami, Houston, Phoenix, Las Vegas. These markets have the gym count to build a meaningful partner network before any regional brand begins to notice what you're doing.
04
The Competitive Moat
Exclusivity radius + coach endorsement + welcome kit embedding. A brand that has signed exclusivity with the top 3 boxing gyms in Chicago, Los Angeles, and Miami has effectively locked the channel in those markets. No subsequent brand can offer those gym owners a better deal — the existing partner already has exclusivity.

Why This Analysis Matters Beyond Combat Sports

The combat sports gear example is specific, but the pattern generalizes. Any performance gear category where: (a) the dominant brands are stuck in mass retail or pure DTC, (b) there's a defined physical venue where purchase decisions are made, and (c) coach or trainer recommendation drives buying behavior — that's a gym channel opportunity waiting to be built.

Yoga apparel at yoga studios. Cycling gear at indoor cycling concepts. Swim gear at competitive swim clubs. The structure of the opportunity is the same. The brands currently winning those categories on Amazon are not the brands winning those venues — and venue distribution, once built, is harder to dislodge than any Amazon ranking.

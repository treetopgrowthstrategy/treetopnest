01

Who Dave is.

The shortest possible orientation, so anyone drafting under his name knows what they're channeling.

Dave is a senior operator. He's been inside gyms, he's been across multi-site rollouts, and he knows the difference between what sounds good in a deck and what actually moves the needle on a Tuesday morning.

He thinks out loud. He names specific systems, partners, and numbers (Matrix, EOS, Woodway, 330 sensors, 40 locations). He treats every conversation like a working session, not a presentation. He'll admit when he doesn't know something, then move on with confidence.

His writing should feel like Dave on a phone call: fast, candid, named, and never polished into corporate mush.

"I think we're overthinking it. Here's the thing. The low-hanging fruit is right in front of us. Are we ready?" Composite. Dave, several occasions.
02

Six voice traits.

Every line of copy under Dave's name should hit at least two of these. If it hits zero, rewrite.

01 · TRAIT

Thinks out loud

Reasoning shows up on the page. He works through the problem in front of the reader instead of arriving with a polished verdict.

02 · TRAIT

Stacked enthusiasm

Short, punchy bursts of energy. "Cool stuff." "Moving pretty fast." Multiple beats of momentum, not one polished sentence.

03 · TRAIT

Specific and named

Brands, partners, integers. Matrix. EOS. Woodway. 330 sensors. 40 locations. Real nouns, real numbers, no abstractions.

04 · TRAIT

Closer energy

He brings every thread to a decision. "Are we ready?" "Let's get rolling." Always asking the reader to commit.

05 · TRAIT

Self-aware vulnerability

"I don't know enough about this, but..." He'll admit a gap, then keep moving. The admission earns the credibility for everything else.

06 · TRAIT

Casual but credible

Phone-call energy, not LinkedIn-influencer energy. He'll say "candidly" and "anyhow" in the same paragraph as a hard operational metric.

03

Do this. Never that.

The fastest review check. If a draft violates anything on the right, send it back.

✓
Do
  • Name specific systems and partners. Matrix, EOS, Woodway.
  • Use real numbers. 330 sensors, 40 locations, 18 of 40 live.
  • Ask rhetorical questions. "Are we ready?" "Right?"
  • Admit uncertainty when it's real. "I don't know enough about this, but..."
  • Move fast. Short sentences. Working-session pace.
  • Sound like a phone call, not a memo.
  • Use "candidly" and "here's the thing" to introduce the real point.
  • End with a decision or a question, not a sign-off.
✕
Don't
  • No em dashes. Ever. Use periods, commas, colons, or parentheses.
  • No leverage, synergy, best-in-class, excited to announce.
  • Don't polish the voice into LinkedIn-thought-leader sludge.
  • Don't hide behind passive voice ("It was decided that...").
  • Don't pad with corporate framing ("In today's landscape...").
  • Don't write paragraphs that sound the same as every other CEO post.
  • Don't use abstractions when a brand name and a number will do.
  • Don't be polite when the truth is more interesting.

05

LinkedIn, in voice.

Three reference posts. Match the pace, the named specifics, the closer. Length is roughly right for LinkedIn.

LinkedIn · 01 Operations win

Spent the morning with the Matrix team mapping out how we instrument 40 locations with one telemetry layer. 330 sensors, one dashboard, one source of truth on equipment health.

Here's the thing. Most multi-site operators are flying blind on equipment downtime, and downtime is the most expensive thing nobody measures.

We're moving pretty fast on this. Are we ready? Yeah, I think we are.

Why this works: Named partner (Matrix). Real numbers (40, 330). "Here's the thing" sets up the real point. Closer energy. Phone-call cadence. Zero corporate framing.
LinkedIn · 02 Industry observation

I keep seeing operators chase the shiny new modality when the low-hanging fruit is right in front of them. Cleaner data on member behavior. Better retention triggers. A floor that actually works at 6 AM.

I mean, the basics aren't sexy, but they're what compound.

Cool stuff happens when you get the basics on cruise control.

Why this works: Governing instinct ("low-hanging fruit") drives the point. Three concrete examples instead of abstract ones. "I mean" mid-paragraph. Closes with "cool stuff" and "cruise control" stacked.
LinkedIn · 03 Honest reflection

Candidly, I think we're overthinking AI in fitness.

I don't know enough about the model layer to talk about that, but here's what I do know. Operators need three things. They need to see what's happening on their floor in real time, they need to know which members are about to churn, and they need their teams to spend less time on busywork.

Everything else is noise. Right?

Why this works: Opens with "candidly" and "overthinking it." Admits the gap ("I don't know enough"). Then earns the right to be authoritative with three named priorities. Closes on "right?" without resolving. Invites engagement.
06

Email, in voice.

Two reference emails. One outbound to a partner, one internal update to the team. Subject lines stay lowercase and short.

Email · 01 Partner intro
Subject:quick intro · EcoFIT + your team

Hey [Name],

Tim looped me in. I wanted to get this rolling before the holiday week kills momentum.

Here's the short version. EcoFIT is building the operating system layer for multi-location fitness operators. We're working with Matrix, Woodway, and a few others on the equipment side, and we're standing up the EOS framework on the management side. 40 locations, 330 sensors, one dashboard.

The low-hanging fruit for your team is probably the equipment telemetry piece. I think we can spin something up in a couple weeks and have a working pilot in 30 days.

Want to jump on a call this week? I'm pretty wide open Thursday and Friday.

Anyhow, thanks for the introduction.

Dave
Why this works: Lowercase subject. References a real person (Tim). "Get this rolling," "here's the short version," "low-hanging fruit," "spin something up," "anyhow": five signature phrases in a 130-word email. Closes with a concrete ask, not a hope.
Email · 02 Internal update
Subject:where we are on the 40-location rollout

Team,

Quick update. We're moving pretty fast on the sensor deployment. 18 of 40 locations are live, 12 are in install this month, and the last 10 land in Q3.

Here's the thing. The data we're already pulling is more interesting than I expected. We're seeing usage patterns that nobody is talking about in the industry, and I think there's a real story to tell once we hit 30 sites.

Candidly, the part I'm less sure about is the dashboard UX. I don't know enough about how the managers are actually going to use this day-to-day. We should probably get three or four GMs in a room and just watch them try to use it.

Are we ready to do that next week? Let's get it on the calendar.

Dave
Why this works: Status update opens with the real numbers (18/12/10). Mid-email pivot to a forward-looking thesis ("more interesting than I expected"). Admits a gap ("I don't know enough about how managers will use it") then proposes a specific test. Closes with the closer.

Subject: I ship software I can't write

For twelve years, I sold HubSpot.

I ran a partner agency. I implemented it, recommended it, and put clients on it who trusted my judgment. So when I tell you what I think of it, it is from the inside, not the cheap seats.

Here is what I watched, over and over. People bought HubSpot the way you buy a name. They bought the brand and the story, as if the software would log in and do the work for them. It never does. No software does.

And the model, stripped of the branding, is a ladder that only climbs. A "starter" tier that cannot really do much of anything, priced to feel reasonable. Then it moves. I watched small businesses get talked onto an 800-dollar-a-month stack that drifted toward 1,200, for tools they used a fraction of, on contracts they could not walk away from. For that size of company, that is not a stack. It is a toll.

At some point I stopped seeing the tool and started seeing the toll. And I had a plain thought, not an angry one. I could build a right-sized version of this myself. I am not an engineer. That is the whole point.

The first thing I built was Billy, my CRM. A place to see deals and track flow, the exact job I had been renting HubSpot to do. Today it does the work I used to pay three companies for. It replaced Pipedrive and HubSpot for the pipeline, and Instantly for outbound.

But building it taught me the thing this is actually about. If I could describe what I wanted clearly enough, I could get exactly that. Not the vendor's version. Not the closest tier they would sell me. Exactly what I asked for. The hard part was never the code. It was the requirements.

Here is where people go wrong, and I went wrong first. The temptation is to build. I once built a product in a single night just because I could, and scrapped it completely the next week. The cadence that feels like progress, think, build, think, build, is a dopamine loop. The one that works is slower: think, think, think. Check, check, check. Vet, vet, vet. Then build.

And the fair question: if I cannot read the code, how do I know it is right? I do not eyeball it and pronounce it correct. I cannot. I make the system check its own work, recursively, and I build the checking into the thing itself. My content engine will not publish a piece that reads like a machine wrote it, because I made it grade its own writing and refuse. It is not magic, and it is not free. It is a different discipline.

What it bought me was not free software. It was a different cost structure. Renting your stack means fixed costs that only climb. Building it flips you to usage-based and metered: you pay for the intelligence you actually use. Fixed cost is what caps how much one person can run. Metered cost does not.

The software still will not work itself, and it never will. But for the first time, I do not need it to. I did not learn to code. I learned to deploy intelligence in a way that lets me own my own tech stack and bring more customized solutions to the brands I work with.

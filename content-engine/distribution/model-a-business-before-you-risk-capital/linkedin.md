Most people use AI to write faster. The highest-leverage move is the opposite.

For two years I aimed it at the wrong problem. Draft this, rewrite that. It made me quicker, not smarter.

Then I put a real deal in front of it and asked it to argue with me.

Rebuild the model. Question the revenue line. Show me where this only works if three optimistic things happen at once.

Twenty minutes later I had a cleaner picture of that business than two calls with the people selling it to me. The bet did not survive its own math.

That is the point. It was cheaper to kill it on a screen than in the market.

Modeling a bet used to be a chore. An afternoon in a spreadsheet you half trusted, surfacing numbers you would rather not see. So most of us skipped it and called a gut read "conviction."

The cost of that chore just collapsed. The math was never the bottleneck. The friction was, and the friction is mostly gone.

The tool does not make the decision. You still own the assumptions: what ramp is real, what your time is worth, whether the market is open or just looks open. It removes the excuse to not look.

Before you commit capital, make the bet argue for its life first.

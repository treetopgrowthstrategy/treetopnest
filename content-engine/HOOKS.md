# HOOKS

How a repurposed unit earns the click without betraying the voice. This extends VOICE.md, it does not replace it. Every hook still obeys the VOICE guardrails and passes the humanness gate (HUMANNESS.md). Read both first.

Adapted from Justin Welsh's post craft (the single highest-leverage lever in short-form), with one hard change for Treetop: Welsh throws rocks at a "relatable enemy," and often that enemy is the reader. We never do that. See the guardrail below.

## The one guardrail that overrides everything

**Throw rocks at a situation, a status quo, or a market condition. Never at the reader.**

Bill is the guide, the reader is the hero (StoryBrand, see VOICE.md). A hook can name a painful truth, but the pain is a shared condition, not the reader's personal failing.

- Allowed: "Most SMBs pay enterprise prices for software they half-use." (rocks at the market)
- Allowed, first person: "For two years I used AI to write faster. I was aiming it at the wrong problem." (rocks at my past self)
- Banned: "You're overpaying because you never read your contract." (rocks at the reader)
- Banned: anything on the VOICE.md banned list ("the old way is dead," "you're doing it wrong," "stop doing X," death-of-category). The humanness gate auto-fails these.

If you cannot tell whether a hook blames the reader, rewrite it as something a market did, or something I learned.

## The fold (the Trailer)

On LinkedIn and X, only the first two or three lines show before "see more." That is the whole game.

1. **Line one breaks the scroll.** A concrete claim, a number, a small confession. Never a warm-up ("I've been thinking about..."). Never a question as line one.
2. **Line two builds tension or narrows it.** Add the stakes or the specific.
3. **The last line before the fold promises a payoff.** The reader should feel they lose something by not clicking. Do not resolve it above the fold.

White space is a tool. One idea per line. Short lines.

## Hook patterns (with Bill-voice examples)

Pull from the essay's actual argument. Do not manufacture drama the piece does not earn.

- **The hidden-cost reveal.** "Most SMBs pay enterprise prices for software they half-use." Then: the tally nobody runs.
- **The reframe.** "That is not a budget problem. It is a leadership problem." Name what it is not, then what it is.
- **The counterintuitive rule.** "In relationship businesses, you cannot buy your way in." State it flat, no hedging.
- **The specific confession.** "I watched a business fail on a screen in 20 minutes. It saved me a six-figure mistake." First person, concrete, a real stake.
- **The status flip.** "The client nobody would take is now the best seat in the house." Old assumption, then the turn.
- **The number.** A real figure the reader did not expect, tied to a claim. No invented stats (HUMANNESS.md forbids them).

## PAIPS, adapted

Welsh's story structure, with the guardrail applied to "Agitate."

- **Pain:** name the shared condition (not the reader's fault).
- **Agitate:** show the cost of the condition, still aimed at the situation.
- **Intrigue:** hint there is a better way you found.
- **Positive future:** show what it looks like once solved.
- **Solution:** the actual move, concrete.

## The close (call-to-conversation)

End social units with one genuine invitation to reply. Not engagement bait.

- A real question the reader has a view on: "Where does this break for you?"
- A contrarian prompt: "Tell me where I am wrong on this."
- An offer: "Ask me the version of this for your business and I will answer in the comments."

Avoid: "Agree?", "Thoughts?", "Repost if you agree," "Unpopular opinion:", fake vulnerability, and anything that reads as farming the algorithm.

## Anti-patterns (auto-reject)

- Any hook that blames or corrects the reader.
- Em dashes or en dashes (gate auto-fail).
- The VOICE.md banned constructions (gate auto-fail).
- AI filler in the hook ("unlock," "in today's landscape," "game-changer"). The gate deducts for these.
- A question as the very first line.
- Resolving the payoff above the fold.

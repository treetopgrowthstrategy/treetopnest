## Week 2: Tool selection & vendor pragmatism (cluster: comparisons)

### POST 6: Monday, Week 2
**Claude vs Cursor is the wrong comparison.**

Body:

These tools solve different problems. Claude is a reasoning and writing partner. Cursor is an AI-native code editor. Engineers should use both.

The right comparisons are:

Claude vs ChatGPT vs Gemini for general-purpose reasoning. (Mostly preference + ecosystem fit.)

Cursor vs Copilot for in-editor coding. (Different IDE shape.)

Claude vs Perplexity for research vs synthesis. (Different jobs entirely.)

People asking "Claude or Cursor" are usually asking the wrong question. The right one is: which interface do I want for which part of my day?

Full breakdown: https://treetopgrowthstrategy.com/claude-vs-cursor

The harder lesson: stop trying to consolidate to one AI tool. The seat costs are small relative to the friction of using the wrong tool for the job.

### POST 7: Tuesday, Week 2
**ChatGPT vs Gemini, in one sentence:**

Body:

If your team lives in Google Workspace, use Gemini.

If your team is on anything else, use ChatGPT.

That is 90 percent of the decision. The remaining 10 percent is whether you need the broadest third-party ecosystem (ChatGPT) or the deepest in-workspace integration (Gemini).

Both are at the frontier as of June 2026. The model-quality differences are smaller than the integration friction differences.

Switching office suites to chase a different AI is almost never worth it.

Full comparison: https://treetopgrowthstrategy.com/chatgpt-vs-gemini

The mistake I see most often: standardizing on the AI that one influential person likes, instead of the one that matches the suite the company already runs on.

### POST 9: Thursday, Week 2
**Zapier vs Make is mostly a team-technical-depth question.**

Body:

Zapier: largest app library, lowest learning curve, simplest model. Right for most non-technical teams.

Make: more powerful logic (branching, loops, error handling), better unit economics at high volume, visual flow builder. Right for technical operators.

The same workflow on both can cost 3-5x the difference because of Zapier's per-task pricing versus Make's per-operation pricing at scale.

Most companies start with Zapier and stay there. The ones that switch usually do it after their workflows get complex enough that "trigger and actions" stops being expressive enough.

Full comparison: https://treetopgrowthstrategy.com/zapier-vs-make

The tool you should pick is the one your team can actually use. Power features in unused tools are decoration.

## Week 3: AI agents & operating model (cluster: ai-agents-for-X)

### POST 11: Monday, Week 3
**Two ways AI agents go wrong in B2B companies in 2026.**

Body:

First: deploying agents on the wrong workflow. The right shape is repeatable, document-heavy, and forgiving of a quick human review. Lead qualification, content production, customer-success triage. Wrong shape: judgment-only work or anything where the cost of being wrong is high.

Second: deploying without a named owner. Agents drift in 90 days without someone reviewing outputs and iterating prompts. The owner does not need to be senior. They need to be named.

If both are right, the ROI is usually obvious within 60 days.

Full operating model: https://treetopgrowthstrategy.com/ai-agents-for-business

The cleanest measurement is process metrics first (response time, output volume), business metrics second (conversion, retention). Move them in order.

### POST 13: Wednesday, Week 3
**Customer-service AI is the highest-volume agent use case and the easiest one to get wrong.**

Body:

Done right, agents resolve the 80 percent of routine inquiries (shipping, order status, password reset). Humans handle the harder 20 percent. CSAT goes up because humans now have more time per case.

Done wrong, the chatbot hides problems behind a flow that frustrates everyone, and CSAT drops.

The difference is the operating model. Specifically:

1. The agent has a documented fallback to human when confidence is low.
2. The agent never improvises an answer. It only cites the knowledge base.
3. There is a weekly review of where the agent failed.
4. Sensitive cases (refunds, escalations) route to human immediately.

Full CS-agent playbook: https://treetopgrowthstrategy.com/ai-agents-for-customer-service

Skip any of those four and the chatbot becomes a complaint.

### POST 14: Thursday, Week 3
**The healthcare AI playbook is HIPAA first, capability second.**

Body:

The vendor question matters more than the model question. Consumer AI tools cannot touch PHI without a Business Associate Agreement. The cost-savings story disappears the first time a regulator asks for an audit trail and your vendor cannot produce one.

The right starting move:

1. Pick vendors with BAAs in place. Anthropic via AWS Bedrock with BAA. Azure OpenAI Healthcare. The healthcare-native ambient documentation tools (Abridge, Suki, Augmedix).

2. Deploy on the administrative work first. Prior auth, billing, scheduling. The compliance posture is easier and the ROI is fast.

3. Move to clinical-support work (documentation drafts under clinician sign-off) only after the administrative wins are visible.

Never let AI make clinical decisions.

Full healthcare-AI agent playbook: https://treetopgrowthstrategy.com/ai-agents-for-healthcare

The clinicians who win in this wave are the ones who use AI to reclaim their evenings. Documentation burden, not diagnosis.

**A solo founder running every function with AI agents looks like a small team.**

Body:

The math works at the seam between solo and first hire.

Marketing content: agent + founder approval. 5 to 10 hours per week.

When agent leverage caps out, hire. Usually somewhere between $500K and $2M ARR for a solo SaaS or services founder.

**A fintech CRO has one job before deploying any AI tool: read the data-handling page.**
